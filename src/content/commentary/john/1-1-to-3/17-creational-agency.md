The three loci before this one developed the predications of verse 1 — the Word's eternal existence, his personal fellowship with God, his full deity. The unit's theology now leaves the verse-1 clauses and turns to verse 3, from who the Word is to what the Word did. The exegesis of verse 3 was kept within the Fourth Gospel: it established, on the unit's own grammar, that [all things came to be through the personally-distinct divine agent](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/11-panta-di-autou/) — the διά read as genuine agency, the Philonic instrumental Logos foreclosed — and that [the absolute negative forecloses every exception, mediating instrument, and competing agency](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/12-oude-hen/). This locus develops what that predication confesses, and the development opens three registers the exegesis deliberately reserved: the διά-agency the Prologue states is not a Johannine novelty but the shared confession of the apostolic church; it grounds a systematic doctrine of creation as the one Trinitarian work in which the Son is the agent through whom; and it was so received across the tradition and fixed in the church's creed. The locus carries the intra-Johannine clause to its inter-canonical, systematic, and conciliar yield. The theology develops the exegesis; it does not re-derive it.

## The apostolic creation-Christology

The creation-agency John predicates of the Word recurs, in nearly the same grammatical form, across independent streams of the apostolic witness, and that convergence is the strongest evidence that the confession is not a Johannine development but a primitive datum of the church's faith. Paul, in the confession of 1 Corinthians 8:6, sets the one Lord Jesus Christ as the one δι' οὗ τὰ πάντα — through whom are all things — alongside the one God the Father ἐξ οὗ τὰ πάντα, distributing the prepositions so that the Son is the agent through whom the all exists. The Colossian hymn extends the predication in every direction: ἐν αὐτῷ ἐκτίσθη τὰ πάντα, in him all things were created; τὰ πάντα δι' αὐτοῦ καὶ εἰς αὐτὸν ἔκτισται, all things created through him and for him; and in him all things hold together (Col 1:16–17). Hebrews names the Son the one δι' οὗ … ἐποίησεν τοὺς αἰῶνας — through whom God made the ages — who upholds all things by his word of power (Heb 1:2–3). And the Apocalypse names the exalted Christ ἡ ἀρχὴ τῆς κτίσεως τοῦ θεοῦ, the origin of God's creation (Rev 3:14). The διά-formula of John 1:3 is the predication these texts share: the Son is the agent through whom all things came to be.

That the pre-existent Christ created everything is, as Carson observes, a common theme in the New Testament — even though the title "Word" for it is confined to the present passage — and the antiquity of that confession is the point on which the locus turns. Carson rests it on Bruce, who, finding no literary dependence among the parallel passages, judges the teaching they share "antecedent to them all and therefore impressively primitive."[^antiquity] The confession is, that is, more primitive than any single one of its written occurrences: the common faith of the apostolic church before it was the Fourth Evangelist's. Were the creation-agency of John 1:3 standing alone, it might be read as the Fourth Evangelist's distinctive elaboration; standing as it does within a convergence of Pauline, Hebraic, and apocalyptic confession, it is shown to be the common faith of the apostolic church, predicated of Christ in independent settings and in a shared formula. John radicalizes the Wisdom and Genesis vocabulary at the level of the agent, as [the exegesis showed](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/11-panta-di-autou/); but the predication that the agent is the Son is not his alone. The Prologue states, at its threshold, what the whole apostolic witness confessed.

Bavinck reads the same convergence from the dogmatic register and gathers it as a single biblical-theological line. The Old Testament, he observes, repeatedly states that God created all things by his Word and established the earth by Wisdom, and represents that Wisdom personally — present with God before creation, the master-worker at his side (Prov 8:22–31). The New Testament elaborates that line: it teaches not only that God created all things by the Son but names Christ the firstborn of all creation (Col 1:15) and ἡ ἀρχὴ τῆς κτίσεως τοῦ θεοῦ, the origin of God's creation (Rev 3:14), the one for whom all things have been created. In all these passages, Bavinck concludes, Christ bears both soteriological and cosmological significance: he is not only the mediator of re-creation but the mediator of creation.[^bavinckline] The reading from the Reformed-dogmatic register corroborates the exegesis: Bavinck traces the same movement from the Word and Wisdom of the Old Testament to the personally-named Son of the apostolic confession, and reads John 1:3 not as an isolated creation-notice but as one statement of the church's common creation-Christology.

## The Father creates through the Son

On this apostolic foundation the systematic doctrine of creation rests, and the unit's predications fix its shape. The two guards the exegesis set on the verb — [the personal distinction](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/15-personal-fellowship/) that keeps the agency from being the Father acting through a mode, and [the divine nature](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/16-full-deity/) that keeps it from being a creature making creatures — are the seed of the doctrine that creation is the one undivided work of the one God in the distinct agency of the persons: the Father creates through the Son. Calvin reads verse 3 exactly so. Having affirmed the Word's deity, the Evangelist now proves that deity from his works; and the διά is the Son's distinguishing mark, for whenever the Son is compared with the Father, this is the ordinary mode of expression — that the Father made all things by the Son, and that all things are by God through the Son.[^calvinagency] The διά is not the mark of a lesser maker but of the personally-distinct divine agent acting in concert with the Father; creation is from the Father, through the Son, as the apostolic distribution of the prepositions already confessed (1 Cor 8:6).

Bavinck supplies the doctrinal frame and its negative guards. Holy Scripture teaches that the Triune God is the author of creation, and — in his exact phrase — "Scripture knows no intermediate beings." It forecloses the Gnostic aeons proceeding from God to play a creative role; the Arian construction of the Son as an intermediate being between Creator and creature who, though created, nevertheless himself creates; and the medieval willingness to grant the creature a cooperative role in the act of creation. Reformed theology, he notes — more than the Catholic and Lutheran traditions — resisted every tendency to commingle the Creator and the creature, and confessed that the act of creation is attributed in Scripture to God alone.[^intermediate] This is the divine-nature guard stated as doctrine: the διά cannot name a created intermediary, for to create is incommunicably divine. And it is held together with the personal distinction by Bavinck's further point — that while creation is the work of the whole Trinity, it stands in a peculiar relation to the Son that, he says, deserves independent discussion.[^appropriations] That is the seed of the doctrine of appropriations, by which the one undivided external work is predicated distinctly of the persons. The fuller articulation — the Spirit's part in the one creative act, and the doctrine of appropriations developed in full — reaches past what the unit's διά alone carries, and is held for the dogmatic engagement the locus reserves.

## Creation out of nothing, and the goodness of what is made

[The absolute foreclosure of verse 3b](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/12-oude-hen/) yields two doctrines at once. That not even one thing came to be apart from the Word excludes any pre-existent substrate the Word merely shaped — the pure-creation force the exegesis read in the aorist, ruling out emanation, an original duality of principles, a form-matter cosmology. Developed doctrinally, this is *creatio ex nihilo*, and Bavinck fixes its sense with precision. By creation the church means the act of God by which, by his sovereign will, he brought the entire world out of nonbeing into a being distinct from his own. The phrase *ex nihilo* does not name a pre-existing matter from which the world was made; it means only that what exists once did not exist and was called into being by God's almighty power, so that the preposition *ex* does not designate a cause but excludes a material cause — the world has its cause not in itself but only in God. And the distinction the tradition drew is exact: the nothing of creation is οὐκ ὄν, the nothing that simply does not exist, not μὴ ὄν, a formless matter devoid of quality.[^exnihilo] Bavinck's care reads back onto the verse precisely: the absolute negative of verse 3b excludes the μὴ ὄν as decisively as it affirms the οὐκ ὄν — not even one thing stood already there for the Word to shape. Calvin reads the same negative at the same register: the second clause is no faulty redundancy, he insists, for since Satan endeavours by every possible method to take any thing from Christ, the Evangelist intended to declare expressly that of those things which have been made there is no exception whatever.[^calvinforeclosure] The emphatic foreclosure does the doctrinal work the exegesis located in it — closing every door by which an uncreated co-principle might be readmitted — and Calvin and Bavinck both read the clause as doing exactly that, not merely echoing the positive.

The same foreclosure yields the goodness of creation. The anti-Gnostic edge the exegesis read at the historically-specific register — the indirect defense of the material order against any system assigning it to an inferior demiurge — is, doctrinally, the confession that the material creation is good because it is the making of the good God's own Word, with no lesser maker to account for it. Bavinck sets the historical stage the foreclosure answers: it was in its controversy with Gnosticism, which to explain sin posited an inferior god alongside the supreme deity or an eternal ὕλη, and later with Manichaeism, that the church won its confession of creation out of nothing.[^dualism] The foreclosure that excludes the demiurge is the same that secures the goodness of what was made; the lock holds, as the exegesis held it, at the broader logical register, with the anti-Gnostic instance named at full weight as the most prominent case the foreclosure includes.

## The cosmic Christ

The universal-significance πάντα — creation taken distributively, thing by thing, as [the exegesis read it](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/11-panta-di-autou/), and creation as the Word's own possession — opens the cosmic register the Colossian hymn makes explicit: the Son is not only the redeemer of those who receive him but the one in, through, and for whom all things exist and hold together.[^cosmic] Bavinck names the same double significance from the dogmatic side: Christ's cosmological as well as soteriological office, the one for whom all things were created and into whom all things are to be gathered up as head (Col 1:16; Eph 1:10).[^appropriations] And here Calvin's twofold power belongs. There are, Calvin says, two distinct powers which belong to the Son of God: the first manifested in the structure of the world and the order of nature, the second by which he renews and restores fallen nature.[^twofold] The creation-agency of verse 3 is the first; the redemption the Gospel will narrate is the second; and it is one Son who exercises both — which is what Bavinck means by naming him mediator of creation as well as of re-creation. The lordship the Gospel will narrate in the economy of redemption is grounded in a prior and universal lordship over all that came to be: the one who redeems is the one who made, and his claim on the creation is the claim of its maker.

How the first power hands forward to the second — how the creation that came to be δι' αὐτοῦ becomes, in [its resulting state](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/13-creational-pivot/), the life that is ἐν αὐτῷ — is the work of the pivot and of the integrative locus that gathers the unit's four predications at the verse-3 hinge. This locus establishes the prior power on which the second rests, and stops there.

## The tradition's confession and the creed

The confession was so received across the patristic tradition. The Greek and Latin readers alike — among them Theodore of Mopsuestia, Chrysostom, Basil, and Augustine — received verse 3 as the church's charter of creation through the Word, reading the διά as the Son's cooperation in the one creative work and not bare instrumental service.[^reception] The reception confirms that the creation-agency reading is the tradition's own, not a modern construal.

What the apostolic witness predicated and the Fathers received, the church fixed in its symbol. The Nicene–Constantinopolitan creed confesses the Son the one δι' οὗ τὰ πάντα ἐγένετο — through whom all things were made — and the clause is John 1:3 made confession, the διά of the Prologue carried into the rule of faith.[^creed] Bavinck reads the conciliar settlement as a single fabric: the confession of the essential oneness of the persons and the confession of creation as the divine work stand or fall together, for only the One who is God by nature can be the agent of a work that is incommunicably divine.[^standfall] The creedal clause therefore fastens this locus to the last: [the One confessed of one essence with the Father](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/16-full-deity/) is the One through whom all things were made, so that the creed reads the Prologue's deity and its creation-agency as a single confession — the homoousios Son is the agent of creation, and the agency is the work of no creature but of God.

## The register of the claim

The locus carries the very high confidence of the verse-3 exegesis. The inter-canonical convergence reinforces rather than extends the exegetical ground — it shows the confession to be the apostolic church's common faith, not a single text's claim. The systematic development — the Father-through-the-Son agency, creation out of nothing, the goodness of creation, the cosmic scope — is the church's articulation of what the clause predicates and adds no new exegetical risk; it now stands on Reformed and Reformation primary-text convergence, Calvin on the works-proof of deity, the Father-through-the-Son distribution, and the no-exception foreclosure, and Bavinck on the Triune authorship of creation, the foreclosure of every intermediate being, creation *ex nihilo*, and Christ's cosmological-and-soteriological office, alongside the apostolic, conciliar, and patristic registers in view.[^register] In plain language, this is the field's settled judgment on the Word's creative agency.

What reaches past the unit's ground is named and held, not folded in. The full Trinitarian articulation of the one creative act — the Spirit's part and the doctrine of appropriations developed in full — is engaged here only as a seed; its fuller articulation belongs to the dogmatic engagement the locus reserves. The Creator-creature distinction proper — that the being of every creature is traced, at last, to the will of God — is touched only lightly here and reserved to the unit's integrative locus, where the four predications are gathered at the hinge. And the hand-forward from the first power to the second — from the creation that came to be through him to the life that is in him — is the work of [the pivot](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/13-creational-pivot/) and of that integrative locus, which this page does not anticipate.

What verse 3 establishes and the doctrine of creation now confesses is the fourth of the things the unit's clauses teach the church to say of the Word: that the eternal, personally-distinct, fully divine One is the agent through whom all things came to be — the maker of all that is, by whom and apart from whom nothing was made. The first three loci developed the predications of verse 1; this one develops verse 3. Where they converge — where the eternal being, the personal communion, the full deity, and the creation-agency are fastened to a single point of the text, and the creation that came to be through him is found in him as life — is the integrative pivot the unit's theology turns to next.
