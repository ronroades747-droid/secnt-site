The second clause held the Word distinct from the God he is with; the third clause predicates of that distinct Word the nature he shares — θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος, the Word was God. The exegesis reserved the systematic development of this clause for the present locus, having fixed there the qualitative force of the anarthrous θεός: [the Word has the divine nature, fully and without remainder, the same nature the Father possesses](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/) — neither the indefinite "a god" of class-membership nor the convertible "the God" that would identify the Word with the Father. What that predication grounds is the church's confession of the Son as ὁμοούσιος τῷ πατρί, of one substance with the Father, and the Reformed dogmatic tradition supplies the conciliar settlement at first hand. At Nicaea, Bavinck records, the church rejected the subordinationism that made the Son a lesser being and enunciated the true and full deity of the Son, confessing him generated out of the being of the Father — "God of God, Light of Light, very God of very God; begotten, not made, being of one substance with the Father."[^nicaea] What John predicates by the qualitative θεός, the creed predicates by homoousios: the Son is God by the very nature that is the Father's, not by participation, approximation, or grant — a consubstantiality whose underived character the locus secures below.

## The two errors, and the two guards

[The two guards the exegesis set on verse 1](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/09-three-clauses-two-guards/) are, at the doctrinal register, the two errors the church's confession excludes — what Bavinck names the perennial deviations to the right and to the left of the dogma. To the one side stands Arianism, which denies the Son's consubstantiality with the Father and makes him a being of inferior rank; to the other, Sabellianism, which secures the divine oneness by so absorbing the persons into the one being that, in Bavinck's phrase, all distinctions among the three persons melt away.[^twoerrors] The two clauses of verse 1 answer the two errors clause by clause. The anarthrous form of the third clause, which forbids the identification of the Word with the Father that [the personal distinction of the second clause](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/) had already forbidden, guards against the Sabellian collapse; the full-deity predication, the nature shared without remainder, guards against the Arian reduction. Bavinck marks the single term that secures both at once: Athanasius defended that the Son is not μονοούσιος but ὁμοούσιος with the Father[^monoousios] — not one-and-the-same person, which is Sabellius, and not other-in-essence, which is Arius, but the same in essence while distinct in person.

## What the grammar forecloses, the creed anathematizes

Here the unit's own architecture meets the council's foreclosure at a single point. The Arian formula Bavinck records — ἦν ποτε ὅτε οὐκ ἦν, "there was a time when the Son did not exist" — is precisely what the Prologue's grammar forecloses before any council met, [the eternal-being tense refusing the Word any verb of origination](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/14-eternal-existence/). What the macro-tense spine excludes by withholding the language of becoming from the Word, the creed excludes by homoousios and by its anathema; grammar and confession close the same door. Calvin reads the third clause to the same end: the Word is of one and the same essence with the Father, and yet — against Sabellius — truly and personally distinct, neither the Father under another name nor a second and lesser god.[^calvin] What the Prologue writes into grammar and predication, the church writes into its symbol.

## Of himself in essence, of the Father in person

The two guards secure the consubstantial deity against Arius and against Sabellius alike. But a subtler error survives that security — one that grants ὁμοούσιος, and grants even eternal generation, yet construes the shared deity as derived and on that account lesser. This is subordinationism in its older Greek form: the Father is αὐτόθεος, God of himself, the fountain of deity, while the Son is God by communication — and on that construal the greater belongs to the Father and the Son is God at second hand.[^fons] The danger is internal to the church's own grammar, for once it is said that the Son is "of the Father," the language of fountain and source invites exactly this drift unless something blocks it.

What blocks it is the aseity of the Son. Calvin draws the distinction [the eternal-existence locus opened and reserved to this one](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/14-eternal-existence/): deity is of itself, and the Son, considered as God, shares that self-existence; considered as Son, he is of the Father. "The Son, regarded as God, and without reference to person, is also of himself; though we also say that, regarded as Son, he is of the Father" — so that "his essence is without beginning, while his person has its beginning in God."[^autotheos] The Godhead the Son possesses is not a portioned-out or communicated deity but the one essence, which is absolutely of itself; what is begotten is the person, not the deity. Calvin presses the point against any notion that the Father confers being on the Son's essence: whoever says the Son was essentiated by the Father, he writes, denies his self-existence.[^autotheos]

The reclamation is exact. The very term αὐτόθεος that, in the older subordinationism, marked the Father's superiority over the Son, Calvin predicates of the Son — not as a novel imposition but as a turning of the subordinationist instrument against subordinationism, securing the Son's deity as underived in respect of essence even as it remains begotten in respect of person. This is the both-and the prior loci promised would come home at this locus: God of himself in respect of essence, of the Father in respect of person. It is the doctrinal heart of "fully God," for a deity received at second hand would not be full deity at all.

This both-and is not a balance struck between two rival pressures but a single contemplative act, and Calvin gives it in Gregory Nazianzen's voice: "I cannot think of the unity without being irradiated by the Trinity: I cannot distinguish between the Trinity without being carried up to the unity."[^nazianzen] The second limb does the work here. To distinguish the persons — to say the Son is begotten, of the Father — is not to leave the Son at a lower station but to be carried up to the unity, to the one essence in which begottenness names an order of persons and not a gradation of deity. [The one-directional form of the maxim served the personal-fellowship locus](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/15-personal-fellowship/); its bilateral form belongs here, at the locus on the Word's full deity, because the aseity of the Son simply is the refusal to let the distinction of persons fall away from the unity of essence.

The substance of this resolution is not Calvin's alone. Bavinck affirms that the Father is the fountain of deity and that the Son and the Spirit possess the same being by communication, but at once qualifies the language of fountain: to call the Father "God" in this sense names his being first in the divine economy, a designation of his rank and position rather than of a prior or fuller possession of deity.[^fons] The fountain is an order, not a source from which deity is metered out. Bavinck finds the same in Augustine, who abandoned all trace of the idea that the Father is the real, the original God, holding the divine essence present equally in all three persons and the Father fountainhead as person, not as God[^fons] — which is Calvin's own distinction in other words: of the Father in respect of person, of himself in respect of essence.

## The categorical inflection, at the register of confession

[The exegesis locked the qualitative reading](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/) at the broader logical register, while acknowledging at full weight the categorical inflection serious readers press — that the nature predicated is the one definite divine nature, not membership in a generic class of divinity. The systematic register is where that inflection takes dogmatic form. Homoousios is the categorical claim itself: the Son is of the one being that is the Father's, numerically the same essence, which Bavinck stresses is totally and quantitatively the same in each person[^monoousios] — not a second specimen of a divine genus. The qualitative reading remains the exegetical ground, and the categorical inflection does not compete with it but completes it at the register of confession, for the qualitative reading's whole force was always that the nature shared is God's own and not a generic divinity.

## The inclusio, from the threshold to the confession of Thomas

[The exegesis closed the predication's inclusio at the level of the text](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/): the anarthrous qualitative θεός of the first verse is answered by the articular ὁ θεός of Thomas's confession at the Gospel's close, ὁ κύριός μου καὶ ὁ θεός μου, "my Lord and my God" (20:28) — the two forms operating at distinct registers, the anarthrous required at the opening to prevent the Word's identification with the Father within the relational frame of the second clause, the articular natural at the climax as the direct address of worship. At the doctrinal register the inclusio is the Gospel's own frame for the confession: what the evangelist predicates of the Word at the threshold, the disciple confesses of the risen Christ at the close, and the homoousios the church confessed is the dogmatic form of what Thomas says on his knees. Barth states the same confession in modern form, ascribing the Father's θεότης to the Logos without reserve — the Logos of the same nature as God, no more and no less than God himself, the divine nature ascribed to the Word without dissolving the personal distinction.[^barth]

## The register of the claim

The development carries the very high confidence of [the exegetical lock on the third clause](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/) — the strongest judgment the unit reaches, resting on the convergence of the field's major readers on the qualitative force of the clause — now joined by the conciliar settlement engaged at first hand through Bavinck and by the Nicene–Constantinopolitan confession itself, with Barth's ascription of the divine nature to the Word carried from the exegesis and Calvin's reading of the clause in support.[^register] The development of the Son's aseity — God of himself in essence, of the Father in person — rests on Calvin at the *Institutes* and on Bavinck's qualification of the fountain-language; it deepens the confession of full deity rather than reopening it, for a deity underived is finally what "fully God" means, and it carries no risk the qualitative reading did not already bear. The systematic development adds no exegetical risk: the homoousios is the church's articulation of what the qualitative predication already says, and the categorical inflection it gives dogmatic form was acknowledged at the exegesis as reinforcing rather than competing with the qualitative reading. In plain terms, the full deity of the Word is the settled judgment of the church and of the field that reads its threshold; the locus does not argue it newly but confesses it on the strength of what the clause was already shown to say.

What the third clause establishes and the doctrine of the full deity now confesses is the third of the things the unit's clauses teach the church to say of the Word: that the Word who eternally is, eternally toward God, is by that same nature fully God — of one substance with the Father, the same in essence while distinct in person. The first three loci have developed the three predications of verse 1, the eternal existence, the personal fellowship, and the full deity. The unit's theology now leaves the clauses of verse 1 and turns to verse 3 — from who the Word is to what the Word did, to the creation that came to be through the personally-distinct divine agent, which is where the unit's theology turns next.
