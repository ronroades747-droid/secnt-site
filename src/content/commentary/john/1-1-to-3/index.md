## 1. Introduction

### The Passage

**John 1:1–3 (NA28)**

> Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν, καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος. οὗτος ἦν ἐν ἀρχῇ πρὸς τὸν θεόν. πάντα δι' αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο, καὶ χωρὶς αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο οὐδὲ ἕν ὃ γέγονεν.

> *(En archē ēn ho logos, kai ho logos ēn pros ton theon, kai theos ēn ho logos. houtos ēn en archē pros ton theon. panta di' autou egeneto, kai chōris autou egeneto oude hen ho gegonen.)*

**John 1:1–3 (BSB, with text-critical adjustment)**

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was with God in the beginning. Through Him all things were made, and without Him nothing was made.

Per the project's text-critical decisions (settled in the discussion of the text), this commentary reads *oude hen* (rather than *ouden*) at the close of v. 3a and places the full stop after *oude hen* (Reading A), with *ho gegonen* opening v. 4 rather than closing v. 3. The BSB's rendering, which reads *ouden* and closes v. 3 with "that has been made" under Reading B, is given here adjusted to the working text. Both adjustments — the form-question at 1:3b and the punctuation at 1:3/4 — converge on the same reading of v. 3 as a self-contained creational statement bounded at *oude hen*, with *ho gegonen* operating across the verse-boundary as the v. 3 → v. 4 architectural pivot.

### Why This Passage Matters

The Gospel of John opens not with a birth narrative, not with a genealogy, not with the beginning of a ministry — but with eternity. *En archē ēn ho logos.* In the beginning was the Word. Before the first creative act, before the first day, before time itself had its start — the Word already was.

Three verses, forty-three Greek words, and the entire christological framework within which everything that follows in the Gospel is to be read. Verse 1 establishes the Word's eternal existence, the Word's personal fellowship with God, and the Word's full deity, in three clauses building on one another. Verse 2 consolidates: this Word who is God is the very one who was in the beginning and with God, foreclosing two misreadings that the v. 1 clauses, taken in sequence, might invite. Verse 3 draws out the first and most immediate implication: this Word is the one through whom all things came into being, with the scope of his creative agency rendered absolute by a double-negation construction that admits no exceptions. The unit closes at v. 3 with creation complete and the universe in being, then pivots at the v. 3 → v. 4 boundary from the eternal *ēn*-state of the Word to the resulting-state of created reality in Him.

The Prologue is the architecturally-foundational unit of the Fourth Gospel. The compositional architecture is not incidental. It is deliberate, load-bearing, and the foundation on which the Gospel's subsequent christological argument rests. John 1:1–3 is the architectural opening: the place where the Word's eternal ontology, the Word's relation to God, the Word's deity, and the Word's creational agency are established as the framework within which the Gospel's narrative will unfold.

What follows is not a sermon on these three verses; it is the scholarship from which a sermon could be preached. The commentary works from the Greek text outward to its theological claims, then names the canonical horizons within which those claims must finally be heard. Each conclusion shows its source engagement so the reader can trace the reasoning rather than receive the conclusion as assertion.

## 2. Framework Inheritances

The commentary inherits six standing commitments from the project's framework for John 1:1–3. These commitments shape how the unit has been researched and how the commentary is framed. Naming them here is not a methodological aside; it is part of the commentary's scholarly disclosure. The reader is given the framework under which the unit has been read.

### 2.1 Linear architecture

The Prologue is read under the Ridderbos/Morris three-movement linear architecture: 1:1–5 (the Word in the beginning), 1:6–13 (the Word coming as light), 1:14–18 (the Word's glory in the flesh). The 1:1–3 unit functions as the opening of the first movement: the establishment of the Word's eternal ontology, the Word's relation to God, the Word's deity, and the Word's creational agency, with the v. 3 → v. 4 boundary pivoting from the eternal *ēn*-state to the resulting-state of created reality in Him.

The chiastic alternative — most prominently Culpepper, Boismard, and Staley, with various centers (1:12–13 or 1:14) — is acknowledged as a serious alternative held by serious readers. Under a chiastic reading, 1:1–3 functions as one limb of a structural pair (typically 1:1–5 / 1:14–18) rather than as the opening of a linear movement. The linear reading is held by serious Tier 1 commentators (Ridderbos, Morris, Carson) and is widely defensible. It is reinforced at the structural-architectural register by the foyer-bracket pattern and by the macro-tense diachronic spine, both of which are coherent with the linear architecture and would require substantial revision under a chiastic reading. The architectural commitment will be tested deliberately at 1:14 (the natural pivot point in most chiastic schemes); until then, the linear framework stands as the working architecture for this unit.

### 2.2 Foyer reading, with the strong (genesis) form held provisionally

The Prologue is read under Carson's foyer image: the entrance hall through which the reader walks before stepping into the Gospel proper, where what is announced shapes how everything that follows is seen. The strong form of the claim — that the Prologue is not only microcosm but thematic genesis, originating rather than merely summarizing the Gospel's themes — is held provisionally. The 1:1–3 unit contributes substantial cumulative evidence for the strong form: the v. 1 / v. 18 *kolpos*-bracket (the eternal pre-existence of v. 1 and the Father's bosom of v. 18 frame the entire Gospel); the v. 3 / v. 18 creator-and-exegete bracket (the Word's creational agency and the Word's revelatory function frame the Gospel's redemptive narrative); the macro-tense pattern's downstream lands across the Prologue and into the Gospel. The provisional rating after this unit's research, integrated with the 1:4–5 unit's contribution, is Moderate-to-high, with the architectural-thesis register of the foyer-bracket pattern still requiring v. 4 / v. 14 pair-engagement at the future 1:14 treatment for full elevation.

### 2.3 Intra-Johannine restraint with permitted forward gestures

The unit's exegesis adopts disciplined intra-Johannine restraint: the unit is treated on its own terms, with forward gestures permitted only where exegetically required. For 1:1–3, three permitted forward gestures are made:

- **17:5** — the shared glory of the Son with the Father before the world existed; the corpus echo of 1:1's pre-existence claim and the v. 1 / v. 18 *kolpos*-bracket's interior content
- **5:26** — "as the Father has life in himself, so he has granted the Son to have life in himself"; the corpus echo of the personal-fellowship articulation at *pros ton theon*
- **20:28** — Thomas's confession; the christological bookend forming the Gospel's inclusio with the deity-of-the-Word claim at 1:1c

The broader Johannine corpus material on the Word's relation to the Father, the Father–Son coinherence, and the Son's full deity (10:30; 14:9–11; 8:58) is engaged in the theological implications where it substantively illuminates the unit's claims. Material that develops what 1:1–3 establishes but belongs to subsequent passages of the Prologue (1:4–5's light/darkness theology; 1:9's true light coming into the world; 1:14's incarnation) is reserved for those passages.

### 2.4 Inter-canonical permission, applied to five touchpoints, governed by substantive illumination

The Theological Implications section engages five canonical touchpoints beyond the Gospel of John where they substantively illuminate the unit's claims:

- **Genesis 1:1–3** — the Hebrew Bible's foundational creational narrative; the *bereshit* / *en archē* echo at 1:1a; the creative divine speech that the Logos identification of 1:1 takes up christologically. Foundational, not optional.
- **Colossians 1:15–17** — Paul's most direct christological articulation of the Word's creational agency; the *en autō ektisthē ta panta* parallel to 1:3's *panta di' autou egeneto*; the *prōtotokos pasēs ktiseōs* register that the personally-distinct divine agent reading of 1:3 inhabits. Required engagement in the theological implications.
- **Hebrews 1:2–3** — "through whom he made the universe"; the *charaktēr tēs hypostaseōs autou* register of the Son's relation to the Father. Required engagement in the theological implications.
- **1 Corinthians 8:6** — the *heis Kyrios Iēsous Christos di' hou ta panta* formulation; the earliest Pauline articulation of the Word's universal creative agency, predating the Gospel and grounding the christological-creational pattern as Christian common ground. Engaged in the theological implications.
- **Philippians 2:6–8** — *en morphē theou hyparchōn*; the form-of-God register; the immanent–economic distinction that the v. 1 deity-claim and the v. 14 incarnation-claim together require. Engaged in the theological implications.

The discipline that governs the permission. Cross-references are engaged where they substantively illuminate the unit's claims, not where they merely thematically associate with them. The five touchpoints meet that test. Beyond these, the Theological Implications work resists expansion; the unit's theology is deep at these five points rather than diffuse across many. The exegesis remains intra-Johannine; cross-canonical material enters only where the exegesis has been done and the theological consequences are being drawn.

### 2.5 Patristic engagement at primary-text register

The 1:1–3 unit is a locus where the patristic tradition's engagement is load-bearing for the project's distinctive scholarly contribution. Three registers operate:

- **The text-critical register** — Gregory of Nyssa's text of John 1:1–3, reconstructed at Roades 1974 (Wheaton MA thesis, supervised by Gordon Fee) and corroborated at Brooks 1991. The supersession principle (set out under the patristic apparatus below) operates here: Roades 1974 is engaged at primary-text Tier 1 register; Brooks 1991 at published-secondary register. The Nyssen evidence bears on the form-question at 1:3b (*oude hen* vs. *ouden*) and on the broader Cappadocian reception of the unit.
- **The substantive-reception register** — Gregory of Nyssa's reading of John 1:1–3 in *Contra Eunomium*, particularly the sequential-argument reception of vv. 1–4 as a coherent christological articulation. Roades 1974 supplies the primary-text engagement; the substantive-reception material is registered in the treatment of the v. 1 cluster and the v. 3 → v. 4 pivot.
- **The Origen register** — Heine 1989 (*Origen's Commentary on the Gospel According to John*, FoC 80 and 89) supplies the Origen material on 1:3's foreclosure language and the broader Greek-patristic reception. Engaged on the v. 3 creational statement at primary-text register.

The Latin-patristic register (Augustine) and the broader Greek-patristic survey (Chrysostom, Cyril of Alexandria, Athanasius) are engaged at the citation-chain register via the standard patristic-commentary literature where they substantively illuminate the unit's positions.

## 3. Sources and Methodological Discipline

The source apparatus is layered, and at every locked exegetical resolution the layer at which each source is brought to bear is disclosed, following the project's engagement-layer discipline. Five layers operate. Primary-text Tier 1 is full substantive engagement with the source read directly at the load-bearing locus. Primary-text register is substantive engagement with the source read directly, but at a non-Tier-1 register — apparatus, reference grammar, or standard-reference work. Primary-text-synthetic is engagement with an introductory or summary articulation that carries the source author's authority but not the underlying primary-text analysis. Citation-chain is engagement through another scholar's citation rather than the source read directly. Adjacent-not-direct is substantive engagement on an adjacent locus paired with apparatus-level engagement on the present question. The reader can therefore see, at every conclusion, both which sources bear on the result and what kind of evidence each one supplies. Where a source is named not for present engagement but pre-positioned for future engagement when its triggering condition arrives, that forward-work posture is recorded as a cross-reference coupling rather than as an engagement-layer.

### 3.1 Tier 1 commentators read directly

Schnackenburg, *The Gospel according to St John*, vol. 1, translated by Kevin Smyth (Herder and Herder, 1968), is the load-bearing modern Tier 1 source across the entire 1:1–3 corpus. Schnackenburg's Prologue chapter (pp. 221–249) supplies primary-text engagement on the *en archē* opening, the imperfect *ēn*, the *pros ton theon* personal-fellowship reading, the qualitative-anarthrous *theos*, the architectural integration of v. 1's three clauses, the resumptive function of *houtos* at v. 2, the universal scope of *panta*, and the v. 3 → v. 4 boundary. Schnackenburg's position is engaged at every clause; where the project's locked position aligns with Schnackenburg's, the convergence is registered; where it diverges (notably at the v. 3 → v. 4 pivot), the divergence is named and the substantive grounds disclosed.

Carson, *The Gospel according to John* (Pillar New Testament Commentary, Eerdmans, 1991), supplies the modern evangelical Tier 1 anchor and the foyer image that governs the commentary's architectural framing. Carson on the act-of-creation versus state-of-creation tense-change at the v. 3 → v. 4 boundary is load-bearing; Carson's exposition of the unit's exegetical decisions is engaged throughout.

Ridderbos, *The Gospel of John: A Theological Commentary* (Eerdmans, 1997), and Ridderbos's *Novum Testamentum* article "The Structure and Scope of the Prologue to the Gospel of John" (NovT 8, 1966), supply the linear three-movement architecture and the principal articulation of the macro-tense pattern across the Prologue. Ridderbos's reading is the substantive foundation of the macro-tense spine.

Brown, *The Gospel according to John I–XII* (Anchor Bible, Doubleday, 1966), supplies the staircase-parallelism analysis of the Prologue's compositional engine and the foundational engagement on the v. 1 / v. 14 structural pairing that the foyer-bracket pattern extends. Brown is engaged at primary-text register throughout the architecture and the exegesis.

McHugh, *A Critical and Exegetical Commentary on John 1–4* (International Critical Commentary, T&T Clark, 2009), supplies the most recent ICC-grade engagement on the unit's grammar and exegesis. McHugh on the v. 1 / v. 18 *kolpos*-bracket is load-bearing in the foyer-bracket pattern; McHugh on the macro-tense pattern's structural-classification function is load-bearing in the macro-tense spine.

Barrett, *The Gospel according to St. John* (2nd ed., Westminster, 1978), supplies primary-text Tier 1 engagement on the v. 1 / v. 18 bracket and on the unit's exegetical decisions; engaged in the foyer-bracket pattern and at clause-level resolutions in the exegesis.

Westcott, *The Gospel according to St. John* (Murray, 1882), supplies the historically-foundational Tier 1 engagement on the v. 1 / v. 18 bracket; engaged in the foyer-bracket pattern alongside Schnackenburg, McHugh, Brown, and Barrett as the fifth tradition in the multi-tradition convergence on the *kolpos*-bracket pattern.

Sosa Siliezar, *Creation Imagery in the Gospel of John* (Library of New Testament Studies, T&T Clark, 2015), supplies the 1:3 → 1:17 *egeneto*-echo identification and the universal-significance register that informs the universal scope of *panta* and the v. 3 → v. 4 pivot's downstream lands.

Bultmann, *The Gospel of John: A Commentary* (Westminster, English translation), is engaged as the principal source-critical alternative-architectural-framework in the foyer-bracket pattern; the source-critical reading of the Prologue as redactional composition is named, its grounds articulated, and the substantive grounds for the project's compositional-unity reading registered.

Calvin's *Commentary on the Gospel according to John* was read directly at every clause-level resolution. The Reformed-tradition register is foundational to the commentary's articulation of the unit's theological implications.

Köstenberger (*A Theology of John's Gospel and Letters*, Zondervan Academic, 2009) and the EGGNT John volume by Harris (*John*, B&H Academic, 2015) are engaged at primary-text register on their respective scholarly contributions: Köstenberger at the theological-articulation register; Harris at the grammatical-decisions register.

### 3.2 Patristic apparatus

Greek-patristic engagement, primary-source register. Roades 1974 (*The Text of the Gospel of John in the Writings of Gregory of Nyssa*, MA Thesis, Wheaton College, supervised by Gordon Fee) is a primary-source patristic engagement at the text-critical register (1:3b form-question; broader Nyssen text of John 1:1–3) and at the substantive-reception register (Gregory's reading of vv. 1–4 in *Contra Eunomium*, particularly the *Contra Eunomium* 3.10.26 sequential argument). The Roades thesis supplies the project's distinctive contribution at the patristic-textual register: Gregory of Nyssa's text of John 1:1–3, reconstructed from his citations in *Contra Eunomium* and across the Nyssen corpus, with the *oude hen* form attested and the substantive-reception of vv. 1–4 as a coherent christological articulation grounded.

The supersession principle operates here. Roades 1974 and Brooks 1991 (*The New Testament Text of Gregory of Nyssa*, SBL New Testament in the Greek Fathers 2) are independent treatments of the same witness — Gregory of Nyssa's text of John — connected not by a derivation chain but through Gordon Fee, who supervised the Roades thesis and edited the New Testament in the Greek Fathers series in which the Brooks monograph appeared. At the Prologue loci where the project author's collation of that witness is original primary-text work, Roades 1974 takes precedence over Brooks's published secondary treatment of the same witness — on the strength of the originality of the primary-text collation, not on any claim that the secondary derives from or builds downstream on it. The principle is bounded accordingly: it operates where Roades's work is original collation of a witness the published secondary also treats, at the load-bearing loci. Within those bounds, Roades 1974 is engaged at primary-text Tier 1 register; Brooks 1991 at published-secondary register. Outside those bounds, Brooks retains normal scholarly authority.

Origen, primary-source register. Heine 1989 (*Origen: Commentary on the Gospel According to John*, Fathers of the Church 80 and 89, Catholic University of America Press) supplies the primary-source engagement on Origen's reading of John 1:1–3, particularly Origen's reading of 1:3's foreclosure language and the broader Greek-patristic reception of the Word's creational agency. Engaged at primary-text register.

Broader patristic survey, citation-chain register. Augustine on the eternal Word and the Word's relation to the *imago Dei* is engaged at the citation-chain register via Ike Miller, *Seeing by the Light* (IVP Academic, 2020); the Latin-patristic register is anchored on the Augustine reading. Chrysostom, Cyril of Alexandria, and the broader fourth- and fifth-century reception are engaged via Joel Elowsky, *Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture: New Testament IVa: John 1–10* (IVP Academic, 2007), at the citation-chain register. Athanasius on the imperfect *ēn* ("there was no time in which the Logos was not") is engaged at the citation-chain register via the standard patristic-theological literature.

### 3.3 Text-critical and grammatical apparatus

Houghton, *A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament* (UBS6 companion, Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft, 2025), supplies the most recent critical-edition engagement on the form-question at 1:3b and the punctuation at 1:3/4. Engaged at primary-text register.

Metzger, *A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament* (2nd ed., Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft, 1994), supplies the committee's judgment on the punctuation at the 1:3/4 boundary — the {B} rating, the absence of punctuation in the earliest manuscripts, and the prose-rhythm verdict on Reading A.

Nässelqvist, "The Question of Punctuation in John 1:3–4: Arguments from Ancient Colometry" (*Journal of Biblical Literature* 137, no. 1, 2018: 175–191), supplies the colometric argument that revises the linguistic state of the question on the 1:3/4 boundary. Engaged at primary-text Tier 1 register.

Miller (E. L.), *Salvation-History in the Prologue of John: The Significance of John 1:3/4* (Brill, 1989), supplies the most sustained scholarly defense of Reading B (the reading the project does not adopt). Engaged at primary-text Tier 1 register as the principal alternative the project's Reading A adoption holds against.

Wallace, *Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics: An Exegetical Syntax of the New Testament* (Zondervan, 1996), supplies grammatical analysis on the imperfect *ēn*, the qualitative-anarthrous predicate at *theos ēn ho logos*, and the genitive-of-source register at *di' autou*.

Harris, *Jesus as God: The New Testament Use of Theos in Reference to Jesus* (Baker, 1992), supplies the most comprehensive grammatical-theological discussion on the *theos* of v. 1c.

Harner, "Qualitative Anarthrous Predicate Nouns: Mark 15:39 and John 1:1" (*Journal of Biblical Literature* 92, no. 1, 1973: 75–87), supplies the load-bearing analysis of the qualitative-anarthrous construction at v. 1c. Engaged at primary-text Tier 1 register, alongside Harris.

Zerwick and Grosvenor, *A Grammatical Analysis of the Greek New Testament* (Biblical Institute Press, 1974), supplies the canonical reference-grammar articulation of the fourfold *ēn* opposed to *egeneto* at v. 3. The project's three-term articulation (*ēn* / *egeneto* / *ho gegonen*) extends the two-term reference-grammar form; the extension is articulated in the macro-tense spine.

Abbott, *Johannine Grammar* (Adam & Charles Black, 1906), supplies the historical-philological engagement on *einai pros tina* as a construction without precedent in classical Greek or the LXX for mere spatial accompaniment.

### 3.4 Reformed-tradition systematic register

Bavinck, *Reformed Dogmatics* vol. 2 (Baker Academic), supplies the Reformed-tradition systematic articulation of the Word's eternal generation, the Son's full deity, and the divine life as one with the divine essence. Engaged on the Eternal Word, Personal Fellowship, and the Word's Deity.

Calvin's *Commentary on the Gospel according to John* was read directly at every clause-level resolution. The Reformed-tradition register is foundational to the commentary's articulation of the unit's theological implications.

### 3.5 Methodological discipline

The project's methodological discipline operates at three registers across the 1:1–3 corpus:

The supersession principle (set out under the patristic apparatus above) operates where the project author's original primary-text work takes precedence over a published secondary treatment of the same witness at the load-bearing loci. The principle is bounded by the original-collation criterion and the same-witness criterion — the precedence rests on the originality of the primary-text work, not on any derivation of the secondary from it; outside those bounds, secondary sources retain normal scholarly authority.

The convergence-with-historically-specific-instance pattern operates where the project's substantive resolution locks at a broader logical register while engaging a historically-specific instance of the broader class at full substantive weight. In the treatment of the *theos* of v. 1c, the project locks the qualitative-deity reading at the broader logical register while engaging the categorical-deity reading as a substantive alternative held by serious readers.

The multi-tradition Tier 1 convergence standard operates at the structural-architectural register. The Prologue's compositional architecture is a substantive claim about the deliberate-compositional register; grounding it at very-high confidence requires multi-tradition primary-text Tier 1 convergence. The five-tradition convergence on the v. 1 / v. 18 *kolpos*-bracket (Schnackenburg, McHugh, Brown, Barrett, Westcott) and the six-tradition convergence on the macro-tense pattern (Ridderbos, Zerwick-Grosvenor, McHugh, Brown, Carson, Schnackenburg), with Sosa Siliezar reinforcing at the cross-section-echo register, meet the standard. The structural-architectural claims rest on these convergences.
