The last page confessed the Word's eternal existence, and closed by naming what that confession does not say: the underived being is not the being of a solitary monad. This page takes up what it does say. The relational reading of the second clause — [πρὸς τὸν θεόν as active personal communion, the Word eternally turned toward the Father](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/) — yields, at the doctrinal register, the immanent Trinity: the confession that God is, in himself and from eternity, a communion of persons, and not merely a communion God enters in the economy of redemption. The Reformed dogmatic tradition states the eternal character of the relation exactly. Bavinck holds that the relation of the Father to the Son "was not born in time but exists from eternity," and that the Logos "from all eternity he participated in his divine nature, his divine life, his divine love"[^eternalrelation] — which is the dogmatic form of the Prologue's own predication that the Word was already, in the beginning, πρὸς τὸν θεόν. The communion the clause names is not a relation God acquired — not at the incarnation, not at creation, not at any point however exalted — but eternal-state, as [the resumptive consolidation of verse 2](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/10-houtos/) made polemically explicit before any heresy was in view.

## Distinguished by relation, not by substance

What distinguishes the persons, the tradition insists, is precisely relation and not substance. Bavinck holds that the distinction between the persons "cannot lie in any substance but only in their mutual relations";[^relations] the persons are "modes of subsistence" — τρόποι ὑπάρξεως — a threefold "self-differentiation within the divine being," marked by the personal properties of paternity, filiation, and procession.[^modes] This is the dogmatic articulation of [the personal-distinction guard the exegesis identified](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/09-three-clauses-two-guards/): the second clause holds a real distinction of persons — not a distinction of essence — which is exactly the church's homoousios-with-distinction. The Word is distinct from the God he is with by the relation of origin — he the begotten, the Father the unbegotten — while sharing wholly and undividedly the one divine essence.

## Calvin, and the seed of the trinitarian grammar

Calvin's comment on 1:1 reads the clause in the same key: against Sabellius he insists on a real distinction of the persons — the Word is genuinely distinct from the Father, not the Father under another name — and against Arius on their consubstantiality, the Word of one and the same essence. He cites Gregory Nazianzen to the effect that he cannot contemplate the one God without being at once encircled by the splendor of the three.[^calvin] [The two guards of verse 1](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/09-three-clauses-two-guards/) are the seed of this trinitarian grammar: the second clause's personal distinction guards against the modalist collapse of the persons into a single subject; the third clause's shared nature guards against the subordinationist fracture of the one essence into greater and lesser. Verse 1 does not argue toward the structure; it presupposes it and writes it into a single sentence — and the conciliar definition is that presupposition brought to dogmatic articulation.

## The eternal ground of the sending

That the eternal relation grounds the economy is the tradition's settled judgment, and it bears directly on the Prologue's own movement from eternal communion in verse 1 to the incarnate making-known in verse 18. Bavinck states the principle: "There could be no distinction *ad extra* in the unity of the divine being, if there were no distinction *ad intra*."[^adintra] The πρὸς-relation the Prologue predicates in the beginning is the immanent ground of every economic sending the Gospel will narrate; [the foyer-bracket](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/02-foyer-bracket/) sets the whole Gospel within this eternal communion — the matter the unit's integrative locus will gather.

## The register of the claim

The project reads the locus at the register the exegesis fixed. It develops [the high-confidence relational reading](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/) and [the synthesis of verse 1's two guards](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/09-three-clauses-two-guards/) into the immanent Trinity, resting on Bavinck's doctrine of the Trinity and supported by Calvin's comment on 1:1. The development adds no new exegetical risk; what reaches past the unit's own ground — the full doctrine of the processions and the relations of origin — belongs to the systematic tradition, engaged here only in its bearing on the unit and carried forward with the locus on the Word's full deity.[^register]

What the second clause establishes and the doctrine of the immanent Trinity now confesses is the second of the things the unit's clauses teach the church to say of the Word: that the Word who eternally is, is eternally toward God — a person, distinct from the God he is with, sharing wholly the one divine life. The clause holds the distinction; the next clause holds the shared nature. That the Word distinct from God is, by that same nature, fully God — the second guard, and the church's homoousios — is where the unit's theology turns next.
