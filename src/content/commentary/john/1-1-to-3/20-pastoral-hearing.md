The exegesis and the theology of the unit are complete. What remains is to hear the unit as those who will preach and teach it must hear it, and to name the trajectory it opens into the rest of the Prologue and the Gospel. Neither task re-exegetes; both gather. The unit was never meant to stand as a self-contained treatise. It is the threshold of a Gospel, and it is heard rightly only when it is heard as a threshold — weighty enough to bear the whole that follows, and open toward it.

## The pastoral hearing

The audience of this work is the minister and the teacher, and the pastoral hearing of John 1:1–3 is not a softening of its scholarship into reassurance but a naming of what the one who preaches it is given to preach. What follows is meant to be drawn down from rather than built up from: five things the unit establishes, each set out with enough of its substance — the images, the framings, the pitfalls — that a sermon or a lesson can take it up directly. Each is a thing the unit's grammar, not the teacher's piety, puts there. The order is the text's own — eternal being, personal communion, full deity, creative agency, and the threshold the unit will not cross — and a preacher may take them singly or in sequence.

### Begin where the Gospel begins

Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος: [the fourfold imperfect holds the Word in eternal being](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/06-en-arche/) across the opening verses, and [the eternal existence the unit predicates](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/14-eternal-existence/) means that the object of Christian faith is not a teacher who arose within history but the One who eternally was and through whom history itself came to be. To begin the preaching of John anywhere later than the beginning is to begin it wrongly.

The temptation runs the other way, and it is worth naming from the pulpit because it is so natural. A Gospel is a story, and a preacher reaches instinctively for where the story seems to start — the Galilee of the ministry, the teacher with his followers, the man who healed and spoke and died. Opened there, Jesus is a great man among great men, and the sermon becomes an argument that he was the greatest. John forbids that opening. He sets the beginning of his Gospel before the beginning of the world, and the preacher who honors that will not let the congregation meet Jesus first as a figure inside history who is then, with effort, argued up to divinity. They meet him first as the One who already was.

The plainest way to preach this is to let two sentences sound one after another. Read Genesis 1:1 aloud, then John 1:1. The congregation hears the same two words open both — *in the beginning* — and then the turn no one expects. Genesis says God *made*; John, at the very point where Genesis sets the first act of creation, says not that the Word was made but that the Word *was*. Where Israel's scripture puts the first thing that came to be, John puts the One who did not come to be at all, already there. A teacher who simply lets those two openings ring against each other has preached the doctrine before he has explained a word of it.

And the whole weight rests on a verb. John had a verb ready for coming-into-being, and he refused it of the Word — four times he says the Word simply *was*, before the first thing is ever made. The point is not that the Word is very old, further back on the same line as everything else; it is that he stands on the other side of the line altogether, the line between what is made and what simply is. To ask when the Word began is like asking what lies north of the North Pole: the words parse, and there is no answer, because the question carries a category — beginning — into the one place the text has refused it. A candle is kindled and will gutter; the Word was never lit. This is an eternity a congregation can put its weight on. The Fathers heard the verb exactly so and left the preacher a line worth keeping: against those who said there was a time when the Son was not, Athanasius set the Gospel's own tense — there was no time when the Word was not.[^athanasius] For the believer in grief or fear that is not a metaphysical nicety but a floor under the feet. The One you trust was not added to the world and cannot be subtracted from it; he is the ground the world itself stands on, and you were made on that ground.

The one caution to carry into the sermon is that this eternity is not a bare and lonely infinity. The Word who simply is, is the Word who is turned toward God — which is the next thing the teacher is given to say.

### God was never alone

The [πρὸς τὸν θεόν](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/) of verse 1 is not the Word beside God but the Word turned toward God, face to face, in [personal communion](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/15-personal-fellowship/) — and developed as the church's doctrine of the Trinity, the clause establishes that the eternal life of God is a life of fellowship: the ground, not the consequence, of the love the Gospel will narrate.

People meet the Trinity as arithmetic that will not add up — how can three be one? — and they either strain to solve it or quietly shelve it. The pastoral hearing of this clause dissolves the false problem before it starts. John is not posing a riddle about numbers; he is telling us that God was never alone. Before there was a world to love, before there was anything whatever outside God, there was a facing, a fellowship, a love given and returned within the being of God himself. The doctrine is not a puzzle to be cracked but an announcement to be received: love did not begin when it found an object outside God — love is what God eternally is.

That announcement is for a particular person in the pews. It is for the one who suspects, underneath everything, that he is loved out of obligation or pity — that God puts up with him, or loves him because a good God has to. The love that reaches such a person overflows from a fellowship that was already full before the world was made. God did not create because he was lonely and needed someone to love, the way a solitary man takes a companion; he created out of a love already complete in itself, and the gospel's love for the sinner is that fullness spilling over rather than a lack being met. A minister can preach a whole evening on the single sentence that *God did not need you and loves you still* — and can show that this is better news, not worse, than a God who would have been incomplete without us.

The preposition itself is a gift to the preacher, because it is the language of a face turned toward another. From eternity the Son's face is toward the Father; and when the Gospel later says the Son does only what he sees the Father doing, that eternal orientation has simply become visible within a human life. The One who is granted, in John 5:26, to have life in himself is the One who was, from the beginning, with the God whose life that is. And here the teacher can say plainly what many in the room assume to be false: John did not argue his way to the Trinity, and no later council invented it. He wrote it into a single sentence — one Word, distinct from the God he is with, and himself God — and [the two guards of that verse](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/09-three-clauses-two-guards/) hold the church's whole confession in miniature; the councils only spelled out what the grammar already carried.

### His deity is the text's own claim

[θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/), read as the qualitative predication that the Word has [the divine nature fully and without remainder](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/16-full-deity/), means that what the church confessed at Nicaea is what the Evangelist wrote at the threshold — and the teacher need not reach past the text to confess it.

This is where a minister most often meets open resistance, and the clause arms him for it. The objection is familiar from the skeptic across the coffee, from the documentary, from the unsettled congregant who has heard both: Jesus never claimed to be God, and the church divinized him generations later — at Nicaea, under Constantine, by a vote. The Fourth Gospel answers from its own first sentence and its own last. The Word *was God* at 1:1; and at 20:28 Thomas, with the wounds in front of him, says to Jesus, "My Lord and my God." Nicaea confessed that. It did not concoct it. Everything the Gospel narrates stands between those two sentences, and they are its bookends.

That gives the preacher a shape he can use whole. Begin at the end — Thomas on his knees before the risen Christ — and walk it back to the beginning: what Thomas confesses of the risen Jesus is what John declared of the Word before the world was made. The Gospel is an arch, and 1:1 and 20:28 are the two stones it springs from; everything between is held up by them, and take either away and the arch comes down. A sermon that fastens the first verse to Thomas's cry has preached the deity of Christ from the Gospel's own frame, and not from a creed laid over it from outside.

John also chose his words against the wrong ones, and the words he refused make a teaching ladder a class can climb. He did not write that the Word was *a god*, one divine being among others; he did not write merely that the Word was *divine*, godlike by approximation; and he did not simply identify the Word with the Father. He wrote that the Word *was God* — the very nature of God, wholly and without remainder. And the payoff of getting that right is worship. Because the Word is God by nature, to worship Jesus is not to set a rival beside God but to render God his due; Thomas is not corrected for calling him God, he is commended. For the worshiper who has quietly wondered whether his devotion to Jesus somehow competes with his devotion to God, the answer of this clause is that it *is* devotion to God. The one discipline to keep while preaching the deity is to hold the distinction alongside it — the Word is God, and the Word is with God; to preach either without the other is to fall off one side or the other of the sentence John built precisely to keep his footing.

### The One who redeems is the One who made

[The creative agency of verse 3](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/17-creational-agency/) grounds the Gospel's redemptive claim in a prior and universal one: the Word's lordship over those who receive him is the lordship of the maker over all that came to be through him.

Many hear the gospel as a stranger's summons — an intrusion from outside, a demand pressed by someone with no standing to press it. This clause changes who is speaking. The voice that calls the hearer is the voice that made him; the One who asks for his life is the One in whom he already lives and moves and was made. Not a stranger at the door, but the householder come to his own house. The preacher can turn to the most resistant listener in the room and say, with the whole weight of the text behind him, that he is not being accosted by an outsider but reclaimed by his Maker — and that the claim was there before he ever considered it.

Calvin hands the preacher a structure he can build an entire sermon on. There are, he says, two powers in the Son of God: the one shown in the making and ordering of the world, the other in the renewal of fallen nature — and they are the powers of one Son, the second resting on the first.[^twofold-power] The same hands that made the world are the hands that remake the sinner; the power that turns the seasons and knits the body is the power that forgives and makes new. A minister can preach the sunrise and the new birth as the single work of one Word, and the congregation will feel the two halves of their own lives — the world they wake up in and the salvation they hope for — drawn back to one source. Preached this way, redemption stops sounding like a rescue mounted by a stranger and starts sounding like what it is: a craftsman restoring his own work, the artist mending the canvas he painted, the author stepping into his own story to save what he wrote.

And that reaches the hearer at the point of his worth. To the person who fears he is an accident, a stray, someone God has no particular reason to want, the clause answers that he is his Maker's own handiwork, and the claim laid on him is a maker's claim — prior to his choosing, and total. Because all things came to be through the Word, no one the preacher will ever address stands outside that claim. The Maker addresses the made, and there is no one in the room he did not make.

### Preach the passage as a threshold

[The pivot from verse 3 to verse 4](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/13-creational-pivot/) turns from the creation that came to be through him to the life that is in him — and there the unit stops, at the very edge of the life that will be the light of humanity. The last thing the passage gives the preacher is a discipline: to let it stop where it stops.

The temptation to do otherwise comes from reverence, not laziness, which is why it is so hard to resist. A preacher fears that stopping short of the cross and the empty tomb is stopping the gospel short, and so he pours justification, Calvary, the resurrection, and the last day into a sermon on three verses. But the passage is a threshold, and a threshold is honored by being crossed in order, not by being leapt. To say everything on the first Sunday is not generosity; it is a failure of form, and it strips the passages still to come of their own word. The preacher who empties the whole house into the entrance hall has nowhere left to lead the people.

The images that teach the discipline are close to hand. A first movement does not play the finale; the held-back ending is exactly what lets the finale land. A meal is served in courses, and setting every dish down at once is not abundance but confusion. So the sermon on these verses should end leaning forward, with the door swinging open rather than shut: the Word eternally was, is God, and made all things — and in him is the life that is the light of men, which is the next word the Gospel will speak. The congregation should leave not sated but expectant. There is a real mercy in this for the preacher himself: he is not required to say everything about Christ every time he opens the text. The series, the season, the years of a ministry will get there. Faithfulness is proportion, not exhaustiveness — the whole Christ is present in the part, but the part is preached as a part. To preach these verses as a threshold is not to preach less than the gospel; it is to trust the gospel enough to let it unfold.

## The forward arc

John 1:1–3 opens four trajectories, and naming them is the last work of the unit.

**Into verses 4 and 5.** The pivot lands the reader at the threshold of life and light. The ζωή that verse 4 predicates of the in-him locus, and its relation to the φῶς that follows, are the immediate work of the next unit, reserved deliberately. What John 1:1–3 establishes is the ground on which that work stands: the creation that came to be through the Word has its life in him, and it is that life the next unit will name the light of humanity, shining in a darkness that did not overcome it.

**Into verses 6 to 13.** [The creational agency of verse 3](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/11-panta-di-autou/) becomes the ground of the tragedy that follows. The "all things" through whom everything came to be is specified, a few lines on, as the world that came to be through him and yet did not know him (verse 10) — the world that was his own, to which he came, and which did not receive him (verse 11). The universal scope established at verse 3 is what makes the world's non-recognition a tragedy rather than a misfortune: it is the maker, unrecognized by what he made. The witness of John (verses 6 to 8) and the giving of the right to become children of God (verses 12 to 13) operate within that frame, and the next cycle's exegesis develops it.

**Into verse 14.** [The unit's architectural foundation](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/02-foyer-bracket/) flags a pairing the future treatment of the Word made flesh must engage. The life that is in the Word and the Word who became flesh (σὰρξ ἐγένετο, verse 14) stand as a pair within the Prologue's deliberate composition: the eternal life in the Word and the incarnation in which that life entered the created order it had made. The commentary carries this pairing here as a forward commitment, and engages it not in this unit but when it reaches verse 14, where the relation of verse 4 to verse 14 is the load-bearing question.

**Into the Gospel proper.** The unit is [the threshold of a foyer](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/02-foyer-bracket/), and the foyer governs the whole. The bracket from verse 1 to verse 18 frames the entire Gospel between the Word who was with God in the beginning and the only-begotten in the bosom of the Father who has made him known; the bracket from verse 3 to verse 18 frames the redemptive narrative between the Word's creational agency and his revelatory office; and [the pattern of the tenses](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/03-macro-tense-spine/) — ἦν, ἐγένετο, ὃ γέγονεν — runs its three temporal registers forward through the Prologue and into the Gospel's narrative. The deity of the first verse forms the inclusio with Thomas's confession that brackets the Gospel's whole christological argument. To read John 1:1–3 is to be handed the framework within which everything the Gospel narrates is to be seen: the signs, the discourses, the hour, the cross, and the resurrection are the acts of the One whom the Prologue has named eternal, personal, divine, and the maker of all.

Two of these trajectories carry a register the unit does not close. The further reading that the Prologue originates rather than merely summarizes the Gospel's themes, and [the wider claim that verse 3 is the Prologue's compositional fulcrum](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/18-architectural-pivot-locus/), are both held with confidence but provisionally, awaiting the pairing of verse 4 with verse 14 at the treatment of the Word made flesh for their full elevation. The unit supplies substantial cumulative grounding for both; it does not close either. That is proper to a threshold: it bears the weight of what follows without yet being the whole.

The Word who eternally was, who was with God and was God, through whom all things came to be and apart from whom not even one thing came to be — this is the One in whom what has come to be has its life. With that the unit closes, and the Gospel begins.

> Eternal God, who were never without your Word: before there was a beginning, your Word was — with you, turned toward you in the fellowship that is your own life, and himself God. Through him you made all that is, and apart from him not one thing came to be; we among them, who live and move and were made in him. Teach us to begin where your Gospel begins, and not to hurry past what you have set first — to confess, as its first words and its last confess together, that the One through whom we were made is the One we worship. And lead us on, in your time, into the life that is in him; for with him the Gospel begins. Amen.
