Verse 3 turns the tense. Through verses 1 and 2 the Word has been held in eternal being — [the fourfold ἦν](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/06-en-arche/) that refuses any verb of origination, gathered last into the resumptive [οὗτος ἦν ἐν ἀρχῇ](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/10-houtos/) — and now, for the first time, the evangelist predicates a coming-to-be: πάντα δι' αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο, "all things came to be through him." The verse makes its claim in two clauses, a positive predication and a negative that seals it; this page treats the first, and the foreclosure that renders it absolute is taken up with the verse's second clause. The positive clause is structurally and theologically prior — the foreclosure operates on what it establishes — and it raises two questions at once: what does πάντα include, and what does δι' αὐτοῦ predicate of the Word? The scope of the one and the agency of the other together fix whether verse 3 is a discrete creation-notice lodged in the Prologue or the universal claim on which the unit's architecture turns. And both are answered against a subject already fixed: [the resumptive hinge of verse 2](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/10-houtos/) has positioned the agent of the creation to come as personally distinct from the God he is with, before this verse opens.

## The scope of πάντα

The reading is absolute and universal. πάντα is anarthrous and distributive — "all things," every single thing that came to be, with no category exempted.[^schnackenburg] The choice of word is itself a signal. John could have written τὰ πάντα, "the universe" taken as a single bounded totality, or ὁ κόσμος, "the world" — and he does write ὁ κόσμος a few lines later, at verse 10. Here at verse 3 he writes πάντα, taking the totality not as one bounded cosmos but distributively, thing by thing, the sum of all that came into being. Sosa Siliezar reads the πάντα–γίνομαι pairing of verse 3 as creation imagery in its own right — John's own, not a phrase carried over from Genesis, where the two never stand together — deployed to mark the universal significance of the Word in creation.[^sosa] Bultmann renders the force plainly: everything without exception.[^bultmann] The clause stands within a creation-by-word tradition older than itself — the recurring "and God said … and it came to be" of Genesis 1, and the Wisdom literature's vocabulary of the one who made all things by his word. But John does to that tradition what verse 1 did to "in the beginning": he receives the discourse and radicalizes the claim. Wisdom was present at creation, beside God (Prov 8:30, παρ' αὐτῷ); the Word is the agent of creation, the one through whom it came to be. The vocabulary is borrowed; the predication outruns the vocabulary it borrows.

## The agency of δι' αὐτοῦ

The construction is διά with the genitive — "through him" — and the question is what kind of agency it names. One reading is to be foreclosed: the instrumental one, that the Word is the tool through which the Father worked, the δι' οὗ of a being inferior to God, a bridge thrown across the gulf between divine spirit and the material world. This is Philo's Logos, and it is the strongest form of the construal the clause refuses — the agency proper belonging to the Father, the Word merely mediating. Schnackenburg forecloses it at the agency register: he locates the methodological starting-point for John's διά not in Philo's instrumental Logos but in the apostolic confession of creation through the pre-existent Christ.[^schnackenburg] The agent of verse 3 is, rather, the personally-distinct divine agent — distinct, by the [πρὸς τὸν θεόν](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/) of the second clause [reinstated at verse 2](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/10-houtos/); divine, by the [θεός](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/) of the third. Schnackenburg names the relation the διά predicates: the Logos most intimately united to God is here characterized as the Father's active collaborator.[^schnackenburg] Carson names the office — the Word is the agent of all created reality.[^carson] And Wallace, at the grammatical register, reads the δι' αὐτοῦ as intermediate agency, not impersonal means — the Logos "the Creator in a 'hands-on' sort of way."[^wallace] The two guards of verse 1 do their work again, now at the level of the verb: the personal distinction keeps the agency from collapsing into the Father acting through a mode, and the divine nature keeps it from being the work of a creature making creatures. The διά names a distinct divine agent acting in concert with the Father — which is precisely what verses 1 and 2 positioned it to name.

## The aorist ἐγένετο and the turn of the spine

The verb is the first ἐγένετο of the unit, and the aorist names coming-to-be: the entry into existence of what had not been. Bultmann reads it as carrying the pure idea of creation — excluding emanation, an original duality of principles, a form-matter cosmology, any pre-existent substrate the Word merely shaped into order.[^bultmann] Creation in this clause is not the arranging of prior stuff but the bringing-to-be of all that is. And the tense is structural as well as lexical. [The spine turns here.](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/03-macro-tense-spine/) After the [fourfold ἦν](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/06-en-arche/) of the Word who was, the verse states the all that came to be, and the ontological divide between the Creator and the created is written into the change of tense before it is ever stated as doctrine. The Word was; all things came to be; the two tenses hold the two sides of the line apart at the very point where the verse joins them.

## The register of the claim

The project reads πάντα δι' αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο as the predication of universal-scope creation through the personally-distinct divine agent, at very high confidence — the broadest primary-text convergence in the unit. Schnackenburg on the διά and the foreclosure of the Philo reading; Bultmann on πάντα as everything without exception and ἐγένετο as pure creation; Carson on the Word as the agent of all created reality; Sosa Siliezar on the universal-significance reading and the Genesis creation-imagery; Wallace at the grammatical register; with Harris, Brown, Barrett, Ridderbos, and Köstenberger at the inherited primary-text register.[^register] The reading is locked on that cumulative convergence, and the alternatives are held in acknowledged parity rather than folded into it. A restricted-scope πάντα — creation in some bounded domain — and an instrumental δι' αὐτοῦ — the construal in which the agency proper belongs to the Father and the Word merely mediates — are named as the readings this resolution forecloses, not as nuances it absorbs. What would shift the resolution is narrow: a demonstration that John's διά here construes bare instrumentality, a case the convergence forecloses at two registers at once, since [the Word's deity](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/) and [his personal distinction](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/) make him neither a creature creating creatures nor the Father under another name. The creation-Christology this clause shares with the wider apostolic confession — the parallels in 1 Corinthians, Colossians, and Hebrews — and the systematic Trinitarian creation-agency the patristic and Reformation traditions drew from it belong to the commentary's theological treatment of the verse; what the clause establishes here is the exegetical ground on which that development stands. All things, every single thing that came to be, through the one personally-distinct divine agent — and the verse will seal that predication absolute, admitting no exception, when its second clause forecloses every door the positive leaves formally ajar.

[^bultmann]: Rudolf Bultmann, *The Gospel of John: A Commentary*, trans. G. R. Beasley-Murray, R. W. N. Hoare, and J. K. Riches (Philadelphia: Westminster, 1971), 37–38, reading πάντα as "everything without exception" made by the Logos and the aorist ἐγένετο as "the pure expression of the idea of creation," which excludes emanation, an original duality of light and darkness, and the Greek correlation of form and matter — creation being not the arrangement of a chaotic substrate but creatio ex nihilo.

[^schnackenburg]: Rudolf Schnackenburg, *The Gospel according to St John*, vol. 1, trans. Kevin Smyth (New York: Herder and Herder, 1968), 236–238. On the agency of the διά: he forecloses the Philonic construal — the Logos as the instrumental cause God uses to fashion the material world, made inferior to God to bridge the gulf between divine spirit and matter (236–237) — and locates the starting-point for reading John's διά in the Christological confession of primitive Christianity rather than in Philo's instrumental Logos, the κύριος of the Pauline formulae excluding a dependent instrumental cause (237); the Logos most intimately united to God in verse 1 is thereby characterized in verse 3 as the Father's active collaborator, the διά denoting not service but cooperation (238). On the scope of the πάντα: the affirmation "cannot be restricted to the world of man," all realms of creation owing their existence to the Logos, with the observation that τὰ πάντα is not found in John — the totality taken distributively rather than as a bounded whole (238).

[^carson]: D. A. Carson, *The Gospel according to John*, Pillar New Testament Commentary (Grand Rapids: Eerdmans, 1991), 118: the Word "was God's Agent in the creation of all that exists," "a personal agent" through whom God "created everything."

[^wallace]: Daniel B. Wallace, *Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics: An Exegetical Syntax of the New Testament* (Grand Rapids: Zondervan, 1996), 368–369, 434. Under διά with the genitive he distinguishes agency from means (368–369); at 434 he reads John 1:3's δι' αὐτοῦ as intermediate agency on the typical New Testament pattern — ultimate agency ascribed to the Father (ὑπό), intermediate agency to Christ (διά), impersonal means to the Spirit (ἐν or the dative) — the Logos thus "represented as the Creator in a 'hands-on' sort of way," not the bare instrument.

[^register]: The cumulative primary-text convergence behind the universal-scope, personally-distinct-agent reading — Schnackenburg, Bultmann, Carson, Sosa Siliezar, and Wallace as individuated above — with Harris (*John*, Exegetical Guide to the Greek New Testament), Brown (*The Gospel according to John I–XII*, Anchor Bible), Barrett (*The Gospel according to St John*, 2nd ed.), Ridderbos (*The Gospel according to John: A Theological Commentary*), and Köstenberger (*John*, Baker Exegetical Commentary on the New Testament) at the inherited primary-text register.
