Each of the three clauses has now been read on its own; this section reads them as the one argument they compose. ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν, καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος. Read clause by clause, the verse makes three predications; read as a whole, it makes a single claim built in three movements, carried by one subject named once and never relinquished. The subject of all three clauses is ὁ λόγος, and each clause is governed by the same imperfect ἦν, so that the verse states three things of one Word within one order of being: that he was, in the beginning, before all that came to be; that he was, in that same eternal order, in personal communion with God as one distinct from him; and that he was, in nature, himself God. The order is not three assertions laid side by side but a single argument in sequence — [pre-existence](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/06-en-arche/), [personal distinction](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/), [divine nature](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/) — each clause establishing what the next requires and none of them intelligible without the others.

## One subject, one order of being

The constancy of subject and tense is what makes the three clauses one statement. There is no change of grammatical subject across the verse and no change of verb: the Word is named at the outset and held through to the end, and the imperfect ἦν governs each predication in turn. The effect is that pre-existence, personal communion, and divine nature are not three properties acquired in succession but three things eternally and simultaneously true of one Word. And the three are ordered by dependence, not merely by sequence. The first clause fixes the order of being within which the other two are asserted — a communion and a nature predicated of the Word who simply *is*, not of a being who came to be. The second requires the first and supplies what the third needs: a person, distinct from the God he is with, of whom deity can be predicated without that deity collapsing the distinction. The third requires the second and completes it: the nature that makes the communion a communion within the one Godhead rather than the relation of a lesser being to a greater. Remove any one clause and the others lose their footing — which is why the verse must be read forward as a single argument and not parsed into independent affirmations.

## The two guards

The load-bearing move of the verse is that its second and third clauses guard each other, and the mutual guarding is what makes its theology exact. [Personal communion](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/) without full deity would be subordinationism — a lesser being held in eternal relation to God; [full deity](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/) without personal distinction would be modalism — the Word identified with the Father under another name. The second clause holds the distinction that keeps the third from the first error; the third clause holds the nature that keeps the second from the second. Neither guard is dispensable, and neither does the other's work: the communion is kept from subordinationism only by the nature, and the nature is kept from modalism only by the distinction.

The grammar itself carries the guard, in a variation easy to pass over. The God with whom the Word is in communion is named with the article — πρὸς τὸν θεόν, the personal Father — while the deity predicated of the Word is anarthrous — θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος, the nature stated without the article. The articular form of the second clause fixes the partner as a distinct person; the anarthrous form of the third predicates the nature the two hold in common without identifying the Word with that person. Schnackenburg observed the distinction: the anarthrous θεός of the third clause does not simply identify the Logos with the ὁ θεός just named.[^variation] The two guards are written into the two forms — the article naming the person, its absence predicating the shared nature — so that the safeguard against each error is not a doctrinal gloss laid over the verse but a feature of the words John chose.

## A structure presupposed, not argued

The verse does not reason its way toward the trinitarian structure the church would later define; it presupposes that structure and writes it into a single sentence. There is no argument here from premises to a conclusion about the Godhead, no demonstration that the Word must be at once distinct from God and himself God. The evangelist simply states it: one subject, eternally existing, eternally with God, eternally God. The distinction of persons and the unity of nature that later centuries would labor to articulate under polemical pressure are already present in the grammar of the opening verse, held together without strain precisely because they are asserted rather than concluded. What the church would later define belongs to the unit's theology; what the verse establishes here is the thing defined — already entire, in three clauses, before any controversy made its articulation necessary.

What verse 1 establishes by predication, verse 2 will consolidate by resumption: the demonstrative οὗτος gathers all three claims into one emphatic subject — this one, and no other — fixing as eternal and simultaneous what the density of verse 1 might otherwise leave open to a reading of acquired stages. And what verse 1 establishes as the Word's eternal identity, verse 3 will extend into his agency: the same Word who was God will be named the one through whom all things came to be. The verse that has stated who the Word is in himself opens, in the two verses that follow, onto the consolidation of that identity and its extension into the act of creation.

[^variation]: The grammatical variation — the articular τὸν θεόν of the second clause naming the personal God with whom the Word is in communion, the anarthrous θεός of the third predicating the nature the two hold in common — is observed by Rudolf Schnackenburg, *The Gospel according to St John*, vol. 1, trans. Kevin Smyth (New York: Herder and Herder, 1968), 234, who reads the anarthrous θεός of the third clause as not simply identifying the Logos with the ὁ θεός just named. The full grammatical treatment of each clause belongs to its own section; here the variation is gathered as the formal mark by which the two guards are carried.
