The resolutions set out across this unit — the form of the text and its punctuation, the reading of each clause of verse 1, the resumptive force of verse 2, the universal scope and personal agency of verse 3, the architectural pivot at the verse 3 / verse 4 boundary, and the five theological loci that follow from them — were not reached in isolation. Each rests on a body of prior scholarship engaged at close range, and each was adjudicated against the strongest alternative readings the literature offers. This page gathers that record. It names the sources that bore weight on the unit's conclusions, the methodological disciplines under which they were engaged, and — for every conclusion — the kind of evidence each source supplies, so that the reasoning behind a locked reading can be traced rather than received as assertion.

That last commitment governs how the apparatus throughout this Commentary is to be read. The source engagement is layered, and at every resolution the layer at which a source is brought to bear is disclosed. Five layers operate. Primary-text Tier 1 engagement is full substantive engagement with a source read directly at the point where it bears load. Primary-text register is substantive engagement with a source read directly, but at a supporting register — a critical apparatus, a reference grammar, a standard-reference work — rather than at the load-bearing locus. Primary-text-synthetic engagement works from a source's own summary or introductory articulation, which carries the author's authority but not the underlying primary-text analysis. Citation-chain engagement reaches a source through another scholar's citation rather than through the source read directly. And adjacent-not-direct engagement is substantive on a neighboring question while remaining at apparatus level on the one presently at issue. Naming these layers lets the reader see, at each conclusion, both which sources bear on it and what kind of evidence each one supplies — and, where a reading was contested, which alternative was held against and at what strength.

## The Load-Bearing Sources

The sources that bore weight on this unit fall into four registers: the modern commentary tradition, the patristic witness, the text-critical and grammatical apparatus, and the Reformed-tradition systematic theology.

Across the whole of verses 1–3, the load-bearing modern source is Rudolf Schnackenburg, whose Prologue chapter is engaged at every clause of the unit.[^1] Where this Commentary's reading aligns with his — on the eternal force of the imperfect, the personal-fellowship sense of the Word "with God," the qualitative reading of "the Word was God" — the convergence is registered; where it diverges, most sharply at the verse 3 / verse 4 boundary, the divergence is named and its grounds disclosed, and Schnackenburg's own question is answered on its own terms rather than set aside. The unit's architectural framing draws on a convergent field. D. A. Carson supplies the foyer image that governs how the Prologue's opening is read, and his account of the shift from the act of creation to the state of creation at the verse 3 / verse 4 boundary is load-bearing at the pivot.[^2] Herman Ridderbos — in both his commentary and his 1966 *Novum Testamentum* article on the structure and scope of the Prologue — supplies the linear three-movement architecture and the principal articulation of the macro-tense pattern that runs across the Prologue's temporal spine.[^3] Raymond Brown supplies the staircase-parallelism analysis of the Prologue's compositional engine and the foundational treatment of the verse 1 / verse 14 pairing that the wider bracket pattern extends.[^4] That bracket — the Word "with God" at the opening answered by the Son "in the bosom of the Father" at the close — rests on a five-tradition convergence: John McHugh's recent critical treatment, C. K. Barrett, and B. F. Westcott stand with Schnackenburg and Brown on the pattern.[^5] Carlos Sosa Siliezar supplies the creation-imagery register, including the echo between the "all things came to be" of verse 3 and the coming of grace and truth later in the Prologue, informing both the universal scope of "all things" and the downstream reach of the pivot.[^6] Rudolf Bultmann is engaged as the principal source-critical alternative: his reading of the Prologue as a redactional composition is named and its grounds articulated, and the substantive grounds for this Commentary's compositional-unity reading are registered against it.[^7]

The opening clause drew the widest field, as the Word's eternal existence and the sense of *logos* were tested against the fullest range of readings: Karl Barth on the Word as the placeholder whose content the Gospel will supply, C. H. Dodd on the direction of movement between the Prologue's vocabulary and the Gospel's, Craig Keener on the chronology that tells against deriving the Johannine Logos from later systems, Craig Evans on the Wisdom-tradition parallel and its limits, and J. Ramsey Michaels on the temporally-cautious reading of the imperfect.[^8] Andreas Köstenberger is carried at the theological-articulation register and Murray Harris's Exegetical Guide at the grammatical-decisions register into the clause-level exegesis.[^9]

The patristic witness is engaged at two levels. At the primary-source level stands the editor's own collation of Gregory of Nyssa's text of John — reconstructed from the citations in *Contra Eunomium* and across the Nyssen corpus — which supplies the attestation of the form adopted at the close of verse 3 and the substantive reception of verses 1–4 as a coherent christological sequence, notably in the sequential argument of *Contra Eunomium* 3.10.26.[^10] Origen's reading of verse 3, as edited and translated by Ronald Heine, supplies the Greek-patristic engagement on the foreclosure language and the early defense of the Word's creative agency against a diminishing reading.[^11] The broader reception is carried at the citation-chain level: Augustine on the eternal Word through Ike Miller's study of illumination in Augustine and Barth; Chrysostom, Cyril of Alexandria, and the wider fourth- and fifth-century reception through Joel Elowsky's collection and through Schnackenburg's apparatus; and Athanasius's "there was no time in which the Logos was not" through the standard patristic-theological literature.[^12]

The text-critical decisions — the form of the negative at the close of verse 3 and the placement of the sentence-boundary between verses 3 and 4 — rest on the most recent critical-edition scholarship alongside the standard apparatus. H. A. G. Houghton's 2025 companion to the sixth edition of the United Bible Societies' text supplies the current critical engagement, and Bruce Metzger's commentary supplies the committee's judgment on the punctuation, its confidence rating, and the prose-rhythm verdict that favors the adopted reading.[^13] Dan Nässelqvist's colometric study reopens the linguistic side of the punctuation question and is engaged at the load-bearing level, as is Ed. L. Miller's monograph, the fullest scholarly defense of the reading this Commentary does not adopt, held against at full strength.[^14] At the grammatical register, Daniel Wallace's syntax is the reference grammar on the imperfect, the qualitative-anarthrous predicate, and the genitive of source; Murray Harris's *Jesus as God* supplies the comprehensive grammatical-theological discussion of the "God" of verse 1c; Philip Harner's 1973 study supplies the load-bearing analysis of the qualitative-anarthrous construction; Zerwick and Grosvenor supply the reference-grammar articulation of the fourfold "was" set against "came to be"; Edwin Abbott supplies the philological point that "to be with" in this construction has no precedent for mere spatial accompaniment; E. C. Colwell's rule is named directly; and A. T. Robertson's *Word Pictures* supplies the standard-reference note on the resumptive demonstrative of verse 2.[^15]

The theological loci are articulated in the Reformed-tradition register. John Calvin's commentary on John was read directly at every clause and is foundational to the unit's theological articulation; Herman Bavinck's *Reformed Dogmatics* supplies the systematic treatment of the Word's eternal generation, the Son's full deity, and the divine life as one with the divine essence.[^16] Both are engaged not as supporting citations but at full load-bearing weight in the theological development, and both are disclosed accordingly.

## The Methodological Discipline

Three disciplines governed how these sources were weighed.

The first is the supersession principle. At the loci where the editor's original collation of Gregory of Nyssa's text of John is primary-text work, that collation takes precedence over the published secondary treatment of the same witness — James Brooks's monograph in the New Testament in the Greek Fathers series — at the load-bearing loci. The two are independent treatments of one witness, connected not by a chain of derivation but through Gordon Fee, who supervised the editor's thesis and edited the series in which Brooks's study appeared. The precedence rests on the originality of the primary-text collation, not on any claim that the secondary derives from it, and it is bounded accordingly: it operates only where the collation is original work on a witness the published secondary also treats, at the loci that bear load. Within those bounds the editor's collation is engaged at the primary-text level and Brooks at the published-secondary level — where Brooks reaches the more cautious verdict that Gregory "probably knew both readings," declining the conditional the collation supports. Outside those bounds, the secondary treatment retains its normal authority.

The second is the convergence-with-historically-specific-instance pattern. Where a resolution can be locked at a broad logical register while a narrower, historically-specific reading remains a serious alternative, this Commentary locks at the broad register and engages the specific instance at full weight rather than dismissing it. At "the Word was God," the qualitative reading — that the Word shares fully in the divine nature — is locked at the broad register, while the categorical reading is engaged as a substantive alternative held by serious readers, not brushed aside.

The third is the multi-tradition convergence standard for the architectural claims. The Prologue's compositional architecture is a substantive claim about deliberate composition, and a claim of that kind is grounded at the highest confidence only where independent traditions converge on it, each reading the text directly. Two convergences meet the standard: five traditions on the bracket between verse 1 and verse 18 (Schnackenburg, McHugh, Brown, Barrett, Westcott), and six on the macro-tense pattern of the Prologue's temporal spine (Ridderbos, Zerwick–Grosvenor, McHugh, Brown, Carson, Schnackenburg), with Sosa Siliezar reinforcing at the cross-section-echo register. The architectural readings rest on these convergences, not on a single voice.

## The Engagement-Layer Disclosure

With the layers named and the disciplines set out, the record can be read at the level of the unit's individual resolutions. What follows discloses, for each principal resolution, the sources that bore load and — where the reading was contested — the alternative held against.

On the form of the text and the sentence-boundary at verses 3 and 4, the adopted reading — the double-negation form at the close of verse 3, with the sentence-boundary set before "that which has come to be" opens verse 4 — rests on the critical-edition apparatus (Houghton, Metzger), on the colometric argument (Nässelqvist) at the load-bearing level, and on the editor's collation of the Nyssen witness at the primary-text level; the sustained defense of the alternative reading (Ed. L. Miller) is engaged at full strength as the position held against.

On the eternal existence of the Word and the sense of the imperfect, the reading rests on Schnackenburg at the load-bearing level, on the reference grammars (Wallace, Zerwick–Grosvenor) at the supporting level, and on the wider commentary and monograph field (Barth, Dodd, Keener, Evans, Michaels) engaged directly at the opening clause, with the patristic testimony (Athanasius, Chrysostom, Augustine) carried at the citation-chain level.

On the Word "with God," the personal-fellowship reading rests on Schnackenburg — engaged directly, with his grant that the preposition can carry a Koine sense registered even as the relational reading is affirmed — together with the philological point from Abbott and the internal-Johannine usage carried through McHugh.

On "the Word was God," the qualitative reading rests on Harner and Harris at the load-bearing level, on Wallace and the direct naming of Colwell's rule at the supporting level, and on Schnackenburg, with the categorical reading engaged as the substantive alternative.

On the universal scope and personal agency of verse 3, the reading rests on Schnackenburg, Sosa Siliezar, and Bultmann — the last on the sense of "everything without exception" — with Origen (through Heine) at the patristic level and the reference grammars on the genitive of source.

On the architectural pivot at the verse 3 / verse 4 boundary — the unit's fulcrum — the reading rests on Carson on the act-to-state tense-change, on Nässelqvist on the colometry, and on the Nyssen reception of verses 1–4 at the primary-text level, engaged against Schnackenburg's published reading of the boundary at full strength: his question is answered on the ground that later scholarship opened, not set aside.

And on the five theological loci — the eternal existence of the Word, the personal fellowship that grounds the doctrine of the Trinity, the Word's full deity, creation through the personally-distinct divine agent, and the integrative pivot that binds them — the articulation rests on Calvin and Bavinck at full load-bearing weight, with the creedal primary-text of the Nicene–Constantinopolitan Symbol engaged directly and the apostolic creation-Christology of the wider New Testament named where it bears.

The reader who wishes to follow any of these resolutions further will find, on the page where each is argued, the full apparatus at the point of decision. The works named above are gathered in full below.
