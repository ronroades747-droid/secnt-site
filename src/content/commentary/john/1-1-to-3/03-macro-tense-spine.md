If the foyer-bracket gives the Prologue its shape, the macro-tense pattern gives it its movement. Running the length of the unit is a patterned deployment of verb-tense that is not incidental to the evangelist's vocabulary but is itself a compositional design — a three-term temporal spine that inscribes the most basic distinction the Prologue draws, the distinction between the One who was and the all that came to be. The three terms are the imperfect ἦν, the aorist ἐγένετο, and the perfect ὃ γέγονεν. (Shape and spine are coordinate mechanisms; how they are held together is taken up in the coordinate articulation of shape and spine.)

## The three terms

The imperfect ἦν is the tense of unbounded existence: it states what already was, without marking a point of origin, and so carries the Word's eternal being — the Word who, at whatever beginning one names, already was. The aorist ἐγένετο is the tense of coming-to-be: it marks the entry into existence of what had not been, and so carries the creation, the all that came into being through the Word. The perfect ὃ γέγονεν is the tense of the resulting state: it names what has come to be and now stands in being, the settled condition of the created order. Between the first term and the second lies the whole ontological divide the Prologue is built on. The Word does not become; the Word is. The creation does not eternally be; it comes to be. The grammar carries the theology before any predicate is added.

## The spine across the unit

Within 1:1–3 the pattern is laid down with deliberate density. The eternal-being register saturates the opening: ἦν stands four times across vv. 1–2 — the Word in the beginning, the Word with God, the Word as God, and then the resumptive consolidation of v. 2, οὗτος ἦν ἐν ἀρχῇ. Four times the evangelist refuses any verb that would mark a beginning for the Word. Then at v. 3 the tense turns: πάντα δι’ αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο, καὶ χωρὶς αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο οὐδὲ ἕν — twice the aorist of coming-to-be, now predicated not of the Word but of the all that exists through him. The turn culminates at the v. 3 → v. 4 boundary in the perfect ὃ γέγονεν, the resulting-state term that completes the spine's first full statement (the clause is taken up later, in the exegesis of that pivot). And the pattern does not stop at the unit's edge. The ἦν-register is reasserted at the head of each of the Prologue's later movements — at v. 4a, at v. 9, at v. 10, at v. 15 — so that the eternal-being tense recurs across the whole unit as a structural marker, set each time against the ἐγένετο of created and historical reality. The spine is the Prologue's full length, not a local feature of its first verse.

This three-term articulation extends the reading the reference grammars already register. Zerwick and Grosvenor name the fourfold ἦν of vv. 1–2 set in opposition to the ἐγένετο of v. 3 — a two-term contrast between eternal being and temporal becoming.[^grammars] The project's reading carries that contrast one term further, adding ὃ γέγονεν as the perfect of resulting-state at the v. 3 → v. 4 pivot, so that the pattern is not merely *was* against *came to be* but *was / came to be / has come to be and stands*. The extension does not overturn the reference-grammar form; it completes it at the pivot the grammars stop short of.

## The register of the claim

The macro-tense pattern as the Prologue's deliberate compositional spine is held at very-high confidence at both registers on which it operates — the substantive-compositional register, the pattern's structural function across the unit, and the architectural-thesis register, the pattern as the evangelist's intended design. That confidence rests on a convergence across independent traditions: Ridderbos, whose commentary and 1966 study supply the principal articulation of the pattern across the Prologue; Zerwick and Grosvenor at the grammatical register; McHugh on the pattern's structural-classification function; and Brown, Carson, and Schnackenburg at the commentary register.[^convergence] Sosa Siliezar reinforces the reading at a distinct register — the cross-section echo, where the ἐγένετο of 1:3 is heard again at 1:17 — supplementing the convergence rather than standing within it.[^sosa]

The pattern is therefore stated here at full force, as the evangelist's deliberate design, without hedging at the unit level. What would shift it is correspondingly narrow. The two-term core — ἦν against ἐγένετο — is independent of every other open question in the unit; it stands on the bare distribution of the tenses. The three-term form's third term rests on the Reading A punctuation that places ὃ γέγονεν at the head of v. 4 (settled in the discussion of the text); were that decision reopened, the placement of the perfect within the spine would reconfigure. But Reading A is locked, and the convergence on the eternal-being-against-becoming pattern does not depend on it. The residual condition is real but small, and it is named here rather than buried — the body carries the confidence and what could move it, as the architecture itself requires.

[^convergence]: The reading is held alike across grammatical and commentary traditions that do not depend on one another. The grammatical opposition of ἦν to ἐγένετο is registered by Zerwick and Grosvenor (cited above). At the commentary register the same contrast is read as the evangelist's deliberate design: Carson observes that "John repeatedly uses the two verbs side by side," ἦν marking existence and ἐγένετο coming-into-being, and tracks the alternation across vv. 1, 3, 6, and 14; McHugh judges the aorist of v. 3 a "(probably deliberate) contrast to that used of the Logos" in vv. 1–2; and Schnackenburg, on v. 14, sets the Incarnation's becoming against "the ἐγένετο which follows the series of ἦν" at vv. 1, 4, 9, and 10. Ridderbos supplies the fullest articulation of the pattern, describing the imperfects that gather "in sublime monotony" around the Word's pre-existence within the three-part structure of the Prologue. The perfect γέγονεν is read for its resulting-state force — the shift from creative act to enduring state — by Brown, who notes that "the emphasis in the perfect tense is on duration," and likewise by Carson. On the placement of that third term the field divides — this commentary carries ὃ γέγονεν to the head of v. 4 (Reading A; defended in the discussion of the text), as does Brown, while Ridderbos, Carson, and Schnackenburg attach it to v. 3 (Reading B) and McHugh leaves the question open — so the two-term contrast of being and becoming stands independent of the punctuation, and the force of the third term follows on the reading defended there.
