The exegesis closed at the pivot, where the creation that came to be through the Word resolves into the life that is in him. The work of this page is of a different order. Through verses 1–3 the unit established, clause by clause, what it predicates of the Word; here the commentary turns from what the clauses say to what the church, on their strength, came to confess. The first thing they confess is the Word's eternal existence. The exegesis fixed the force of the imperfect: [ἦν names existence without a marked beginning](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/06-en-arche/), and the four refusals of any verb of origination across verses 1–2 — [gathered last into the resumptive οὗτος ἦν ἐν ἀρχῇ](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/10-houtos/) — place the Word not earlier on creation's timeline but outside the becoming-category altogether. What the grammar establishes, the doctrine of the Word's eternity carries: the Word has no point of origin. There is no moment, no "before," no prior condition out of which he came; the eternal-being tense forecloses a beginning to his being and succession within it together. This is the [Creator-side-of-the-line yield of the unit's temporal spine](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/03-macro-tense-spine/), now stated as doctrine — the Word stands with the One who simply is, over against all that comes to be.

## Aseity, carefully drawn

Pressed one step further, the eternity the imperfect names opens onto aseity, and the Reformed dogmatic tradition supplies precisely the careful articulation the step requires. Bavinck grounds the whole doctrine of God in this perfection: God is, in his phrase, exclusively from himself — not in the sense of being self-caused, but being from eternity to eternity who he is, being not becoming.[^aseity] The distinction is exact, and the locus turns on it. Aseity is the negative truth that the Word's being is underived — from no one and nothing outside himself — and not the positive and incoherent claim that he caused or produced himself; Bavinck expressly excludes *causa sui* as a predicate of God, since to be one's own cause in any positive sense is an impossibility. The asymmetry [the spine wrote into the grammar](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/03-macro-tense-spine/) is, at this register, an asymmetry of dependence: [all things have their being δι' αὐτοῦ](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/11-panta-di-autou/); the Word has his being through no one — dependent on nothing, while everything depends on him.[^aseity] Bavinck reads the Johannine confession that the Son "has life in himself" (John 5:26) as belonging to this aseity. And the foreclosure of the Philonic reading [the exegesis already drew](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/06-en-arche/) — the Word not the oldest of created things but standing outside the chain of the made — is, at the doctrinal register, simply the denial that the Word's being is in any way caused.

## Eternity and the exclusion of succession

The eternity that frames the aseity is, in Bavinck's articulation, more than endless duration: it excludes a beginning, an end, and the succession of moments.[^eternity] This is the doctrinal form of the imperfect — not merely that the Word had no beginning, but that there is no before-and-after in his being at all. God is unbegotten and incorruptible, the eternal I AM, being not becoming; and Bavinck lists John 1:1 itself among the texts by which God is shown to have existed before the world was, so that the dogmatic tradition takes the Prologue's own clause as a proof of the very perfection this page develops.[^eternity] Calvin's comment on the verse stands in support, reading the opening clause as the affirmation of the Word's eternity.[^calvin] Here the doctrine reaches [the hinge](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/13-creational-pivot/) the unit's closing movement will gather: for Bavinck, the difference between the Creator and the creature hinges on the contrast between being and becoming.[^immutability] All that is creaturely is in process of becoming and finds its rest only in him who is pure being without becoming. The eternal-being tense of verse 1, the aseity of the divine nature, and [the Creator-creature distinction the spine draws](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/03-macro-tense-spine/) are not three doctrines but one, seen from three sides.

## Two guards from the unit's own predications

Two guards belong on the development, and both come from the unit's own predications rather than from outside them. The first: the underived being is not the being of a solitary monad. The same Word who simply is, is eternally [πρὸς τὸν θεόν](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/), so that aseity is predicated of the one God whose life the Word fully shares, not of an isolated subject withdrawn from relation. That the eternal being is, from the first, an eternal being-toward is the matter the unit's next locus develops; here it is enough that the Word's underived life is a life in communion.

The second guard marks a boundary this page does not cross. How the Word's underived deity stands together with his eternal relation of origin to the Father — the question the tradition answers by confessing the Son as God in respect of his deity, yet eternally begotten in respect of his person — is the work of the unit's treatment of the trinitarian relations, not of this locus. The imperfect locks that the Word never came to be; the mode of his eternal subsistence as Son is a downstream question, and the grammar of verse 1 neither raises nor settles it. The page names the boundary and keeps to its own side of it: that the Word is underived is the doctrine of his eternity; how the underived Son is eternally of the Father is reserved for the locus where the sources that answer it are engaged.

## The register of the claim

The project reads the locus at the register the exegesis fixed. The eternal-being reading on which it rests is held at high confidence, on [a convergence of independent traditions extended by the patristic witness](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/06-en-arche/) and confirmed internally by the Gospel's own speech. The aseity-and-eternity development adds to that no new exegetical risk so long as it stays tethered to what the imperfect establishes: Bavinck supplies not a fresh claim about the text but the disciplined doctrinal form the text's own predication requires — aseity as underived being and not self-causation, eternity as the exclusion of succession, the Creator-creature distinction as the contrast of being and becoming. What would shift the development is only what would shift that tether, and the tether is settled. The one matter that reaches past the imperfect — how the Word's underived deity stands with his eternal generation — is not developed here but carried forward to the locus that bears it, where the sources that answer it are engaged.[^register]

What the imperfect held and the doctrine of eternity now confesses is the first of the things the unit's clauses teach the church to say of the Word: that he simply is — underived, without beginning and without succession, on the Creator's side of the line his own creating draws. The clauses say more, and the church confesses more on their strength. That the Word who simply is, is the Word eternally toward God, and what the eternal communion of the first verse grounds, is where the unit's theology turns next.
