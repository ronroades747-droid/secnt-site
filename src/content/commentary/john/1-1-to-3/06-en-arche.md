The exegesis turns from the text to the verses, and the first verse is not three statements but one argument made in three clauses. ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν, καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος — the Word in the beginning, the Word with God, the Word as God. Each clause is governed by the same imperfect ἦν, and the three together establish, in sequence, the Word's eternal pre-existence, his personal communion with God as one distinct from him, and his full divine nature. The clauses guard one another, and the verse is drawn together as a single argument where [the three clauses and their two guards](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/09-three-clauses-two-guards/) are taken up as a whole. This section takes the first clause, which carries the most freight of the three; its three elements are taken in turn — the temporal horizon it opens (ἐν ἀρχῇ), the title it names (ὁ λόγος), and the verb that governs them both (ἦν).

## ἐν ἀρχῇ — the beginning, and the echo of Genesis

The Gospel opens with the two words that open the Greek Bible. ἐν ἀρχῇ is verbatim the first phrase of Genesis 1:1 LXX — ἐν ἀρχῇ ἐποίησεν ὁ θεὸς τὸν οὐρανὸν καὶ τὴν γῆν — and the echo is deliberate, legible at once to any reader formed by the Greek Scriptures. The opening is one of a series of deliberate evocations of the Genesis creation narrative that the Prologue sustains; the ἐγένετο of v. 3 and its later echo at 1:17 belong to the same pattern.[^genesis] John does not allude vaguely to "the beginning" in the abstract; he reaches for the specific words with which Israel's Scripture begins, and sets his Gospel's opening at the same threshold.

The echo is also, in the same gesture, a departure — and the departure is the point. Genesis narrates the beginning of the created order with a verb of making: ἐν ἀρχῇ ἐποίησεν ὁ θεός, in the beginning God made. John's clause replaces the verb of making with a verb of being: ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, in the beginning the Word was. At the very point where Genesis sets the first creative act, John sets not an act but a state, not a making but a being-already. The ἀρχή thus functions less as a moment the Word occupies than as the horizon at which everything else commences and before which the Word already stands: introduced at the beginning of all that came to be, and introduced as the one who, at that beginning, already was. Chrysostom, whom Schnackenburg follows here, reads the opening words as designating "simply eternal and infinite being" — a reading the clause's grammar will bear once the force of its verb is weighed.[^chrysostom]

## ὁ λόγος — the title and its function

The term ὁ λόγος carries a long and crowded history — Stoic, Philonic, Wisdom-traditional, Targumic, scriptural — and the temptation is to read the Prologue as a synthesis of that history, the evangelist selecting from the available cosmologies a content with which to fill the title. The direction of the Prologue's argument runs the other way. Ridderbos states it most economically: Jesus Christ is the subject of the Prologue, and the Logos is the predicate. John is not telling his readers what the Logos is and then arguing that Jesus answers to the category; he is telling them who Jesus is, and reaching for the highest vocabulary his audience possesses to say it. The backgrounds are conditions of intelligibility, not sources of content — the vocabulary that could receive the testimony, not the soil from which it grew.[^title]

Each background, taken at its own register, illuminates the title's resonance and reaches a ceiling the Word's identity passes. The primary horizon is the LXX's rendering of the Hebrew דָּבָר as λόγος: the creative word by which God speaks the heavens into being, the revelatory word that comes to the prophets, the effective word that does not return empty. This establishes the Word as creative, revelatory, effective; what it does not reach is the personal pre-existence in communion with God, or the full divine identity, that the verse's later clauses assert. The Wisdom tradition supplies the closest structural parallel — a personal divine agent with God before creation, through whom creation came to be — yet at both edges it reaches a ceiling at which the Word categorically departs: Wisdom is created (Proverbs 8:22), where the Word simply was; and no Wisdom figure, however personified, becomes flesh. Its strongest modern exponent concedes the limit, granting that the Wisdom parallels do not finally account for the Prologue. The Targumic Memra is the closest linguistic parallel, but it is a circumlocution for divine activity rather than a distinct person in eternal communion, and the Targums in their extant form postdate John. Philo's Logos is the most developed conceptual parallel — the instrument δι' οὗ God creates, a figure Philo can even name with the anarthrous θεός — but Philo's Logos exists precisely to keep the transcendent God from contact with matter, and the Prologue arrives at exactly the claim that system is built to exclude: καὶ ὁ λόγος σὰρξ ἐγένετο, and the Word became flesh. No synthesis of the available backgrounds generates that sentence; the backgrounds receive the testimony, they do not author it.[^backgrounds]

The title's behavior within the Gospel confirms the reading. ὁ λόγος appears as a christological title twice — at 1:1 and at 1:14 — and never again. Barth names the function: the Logos title is a placeholder whose value is disclosed as the Gospel unfolds and is finally solved in the person of Jesus Christ — the inscription that can be read before it is understood, the unknown in an equation whose value appears only when the equation is solved. The Prologue sets the equation; the Gospel solves it; and once the reality the title held a place for has been named and narrated, the abstract title is not needed again.[^locumtenens] The reading is held at high confidence, on a convergence of independent traditions on the direction of movement from witness to vocabulary; the margin it does not claim acknowledges that the line between communicative resonance and conceptual borrowing is never perfectly clean in first-century literature.

## ἦν — the imperfect, and the two orders of existence

The clause's whole weight rests on its verb, and the verb is chosen with precision. ἦν is the imperfect of εἰμί — the tense of continuous existence in the past — and John deploys it where he might have used a verb of origination. The force is not merely that the Word existed before creation, as though ἦν and the ἐγένετο of v. 3 marked two points on a single timeline with the Word simply earlier on it. The force is that the two verbs name two orders of existence. ἦν states existence without a marked beginning; ἐγένετο, the aorist of γίνομαι, states coming-to-be, entry into existence at a point. Between them lies the whole distinction the Prologue is built on: the Word does not become, the Word is; the creation does not eternally be, it comes to be.[^imperfect] The deployment is structural, not incidental: the imperfect stands four times across vv. 1–2 — three times in v. 1, once in the resumptive v. 2 — before the first ἐγένετο arrives in v. 3, and the fourfold ἦν is set deliberately against that ἐγένετο. This is the opening deployment of [the macro-tense spine](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/03-macro-tense-spine/) that runs the unit's length; here at the first clause the eternal-being register is laid down for the first time.

The Gospel confirms the verb-choice in Jesus's own speech. At 8:58 — πρὶν Ἀβραὰμ γενέσθαι ἐγὼ εἰμί, "before Abraham came to be, I am" — the same two orders are set against each other: Abraham entered the order of becoming at a point (γενέσθαι), and Jesus declares his own existence in the order of being (ἐγὼ εἰμί, the first-person present of what 1:1 states in the third-person imperfect). The parallel is named independently across traditions, and the cross-tradition convergence matters: the reading is grounded in the Gospel's own compositional logic, John deploying the same verb-contrast in his most charged christological declaration that he uses in the Prologue's opening.[^eight]

Schnackenburg states the categorical force directly: the Logos was not created, he simply was — "a real, personal pre-existence," timeless and eternal.[^preexistence] The Word's existence is not before time in a temporal sense but outside the becoming-category entirely. And the reading is not a modern grammatical refinement: a fourth- and fifth-century patristic consensus across Alexandria, Cappadocia, Antioch, and the Latin West read the imperfect as ontologically load-bearing, and the Athanasian deployment of ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος against the Arian ἦν ποτὲ ὅτε οὐκ ἦν — "there was when he was not" — is the imperfect's own force expressed in credal language, the exegesis made confession. The clause forecloses two misreadings at once, and the two foreclosures are independent. The first is the Arian: that there was a time when the Word was not. The imperfect forecloses it — the Word does not come into existence at the beginning; he was already, continuously, without a marked origin. The second is the Philonic: that the Word was the first and oldest of created things. The Word is not the oldest link in the chain of made things but stands outside the chain entirely; the ἐγένετο of v. 3 governs all created things without exception, and the one through whom they came to be himself never came to be. Two misreadings, two foreclosures, the same verb doing both. The conciliar settlement that confessed this foreclosure, and the doctrine of the Word's eternity and aseity it grounds, are developed in [the unit's theology of the Word's eternal existence](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/14-eternal-existence/); what the clause establishes here is the order of being on which that development stands.

## The register of the claim

The project reads the imperfect at this full force — timeless existence, two ontological orders — at high confidence, on the convergence of seven commentary voices from four traditions, extended by the patristic apparatus to a multi-Father early consensus and confirmed internally at 8:58. The margin the rating does not claim holds open the temporal-displacement reading — that ἦν asserts only that the Word existed before creation — not as an error but as a less-than-complete framing serviceable in non-specialist settings; the most temporally cautious of the major commentators presses the imperfect less hard ontologically while not disputing eternal pre-existence, and stands as the evidential floor that locates the reading on a consensus rather than at unanimity.[^register] What would shift it would be a sustained grammatical argument that the imperfect in this specific context — conjoined with ἐν ἀρχῇ, deployed fourfold against the v. 3 ἐγένετο — cannot bear the timeless-existence force, an argument the current literature does not supply.

The two orders the verb establishes are the foundation the rest of v. 1 builds on. The Word who simply is, is the Word who is in [personal communion with God](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/07-pros-ton-theon/) and who is himself [fully God](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/08-theos-en-ho-logos/); both claims are made within the order of being the imperfect names.
