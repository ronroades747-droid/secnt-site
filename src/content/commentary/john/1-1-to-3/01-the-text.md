The exegesis that follows presupposes a working text, and at two points within verse 3 that text is contested. The closing clause of v. 3a — καὶ χωρὶς αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο οὐδὲ ἕν — raises two questions the modern critical tradition has kept distinct, and the unit's reading turns on both. The first is a question of form: at the close of the negative-universality clause, does the text read the two-word οὐδὲ ἕν ("not even one") or the contracted οὐδέν ("nothing")? The second is a question of boundary: does ὃ γέγονεν, the perfect that follows, close verse 3 (Reading B) or open verse 4 (Reading A)? The two are correlated in the manuscript and patristic record but logically independent, and the form-question is the prior of them — the closing word of the clause must be fixed before the boundary that falls after it can be put. They are taken in that order here. The project follows the convergence of the modern critical editions as its working text where the evidence does not decisively require otherwise, and discloses at each point both the ground of the adopted reading and the standing of the alternative it holds against.

## The form of the negative at 1:3b: οὐδὲ ἕν

The manuscript tradition transmits the close of v. 3a's negative clause in two forms. The two-word οὐδὲ ἕν conjoins the negative adverb οὐδέ ("not even") with the cardinal ἕν ("one") to yield an emphatic "not even one thing"; the contracted οὐδέν reduces the same negation to the ordinary pronoun "nothing." The difference is not one of truth-conditions. "Without him not even one thing came into being" and "without him nothing came into being" assert the same absolute exclusion, and the substantive claim the clause makes — the unrestricted scope of the Word's creative agency — stands under either form. The difference is one of emphasis: the two-word form preserves a rhetorical stress that the contraction attenuates. The form-question is therefore not whether one reading forecloses the other at the level of the proposition, but which form the project adopts as the working text on which the exegesis of the negative-universality clause operates.

The project reads οὐδὲ ἕν. The four modern critical editions — NA28, SBLGNT, THGNT, and UBS6 — converge on the two-word form as the printed text, with the contracted οὐδέν registered in the apparatus rather than suppressed;[^editions] the convergence supplies the cumulative external-evidence ground the project follows by default.

**The emphatic stress as substantively load-bearing.** Schnackenburg treats the two-word form as more than a stylistic variant. He observes that the πάντα of v. 3a has οὐδὲ ἕν as its counterpart and that the latter is "unmistakably stressed," asking whether the stress is to be explained on merely rhetorical grounds or carries the distich's substantive claim — the universal significance of the Logos in creation and redemption.[^schn-form] What the form-question takes from Schnackenburg is the prior point: a major commentator reads the emphatic separation as doing substantive work, and the form that does that work is οὐδὲ ἕν.

**The contracted form and the patristic-textual evidence.** The case is not one-sided. The contracted οὐδέν is attested early and across diverse streams, and it is the patristic-textual record that presses the question hardest. The reconstruction of Gregory of Nyssa's text catalogues both forms across his citations of the verse in the *Contra Eunomium* and the wider corpus, distinguishing full citations from allusions, and concludes that the breadth of the οὐδέν attestation resists the simplest transcriptional explanation: if Gregory knew only one of the variants, then οὐδέν "must be considered as original, for it is impossible to explain οὐδέν as the result of scribal corrections over such a wide base," the expected correction running rather toward the Byzantine οὐδὲ ἕν.[^roades] The judgment is conditional, and its force is to establish not that οὐδέν is original but that the contracted form circulated genuinely and early, with a witness-pattern the simple-contraction hypothesis does not easily accommodate.[^brooks]

**Resolution.** The project locks οὐδὲ ἕν as its working text on the cumulative external and emphatic-force grounds — the convergence of the critical editions, the 𝔓⁷⁵ attestation, and Schnackenburg's reading of the emphatic stress as substantively load-bearing — while acknowledging explicitly that the patristic-textual evidence establishes the genuine early circulation of both forms and does not permit a confident adjudication of which is original. The locked form is the emphatic one; the open question is the original one. This is the discipline the unit applies elsewhere at the text-critical level: a working text adopted on cumulative grounds, with the alternative neither dismissed nor folded into the adopted reading but held in acknowledged parity. Confidence is high at the level of the working text and medium-to-high at the level of the original, and the asymmetry is deliberate — locking the text the exegesis operates on is one decision; adjudicating which form John wrote is another and more cautious one. What would shift the working-text lock is narrow: a published patristic-textual re-engagement that takes up the divergence over Gregory's text directly and supplies a third substantive judgment on it; an *Editio Critica Maior* representation that materially alters the witness-pattern; a fresh major-commentary case for the contracted form on grounds not yet considered; or a colometric argument bearing on the form-question as the colometric argument bears on the boundary below. Absent these, the emphatic form stands as the text on which the absolute-foreclosure reading of the creational clause operates — the form that does the rhetorical work the substantive resolution there will trade on.

## The boundary at 1:3/4: Reading A

With the closing word of v. 3 fixed as οὐδὲ ἕν, the second question is where the sentence-boundary falls. The perfect ὃ γέγονεν that follows can be read in either of two ways the field has sustained. Under Reading B the full stop falls after ὃ γέγονεν, so that the relative closes v. 3 — "without him not even one thing came into being that has come into being" — and v. 4 opens with the self-contained ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν. Under Reading A the full stop falls after οὐδὲ ἕν, so that v. 3 closes as a complete negative universal and ὃ γέγονεν opens v. 4 as the nominative subject of ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν — "what has come into being, in him was life." Two further readings have been proposed, and neither functions as a live textual option: Gregory of Nyssa's break after αὐτῷ (Reading C), engaged below as patristic corroboration rather than adopted, and the conjecture that ὃ γέγονεν is a later marginal gloss (Reading D), which has no manuscript support. The project reads Reading A.

**The textual and second-century patristic case.** The earliest punctuated witness to the Prologue carries the boundary the project adopts: 𝔓⁷⁵ marks an elevated dot — the scribal sign for a sense-pause — after οὐδὲ ἕν, and 𝔓⁶⁶ is consistent with the same division. Behind the papyri stands a second-century patristic consensus reading the break before v. 4: Theophilus of Antioch, Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Tertullian, and Irenaeus all relate the passage in line with Reading A. The weight of this evidence is acknowledged even by the position's most sustained modern opponent: Miller, whose monograph is the fullest scholarly defense of Reading B, concedes that the manuscript and patristic record favors Reading A and rests his case for B on theological and linguistic grounds rather than textual ones.[^miller] On the testimony of Reading B's own ablest defender, the textual side of the question runs toward Reading A.

**The colometric argument.** The linguistic side was long held inconclusive — Metzger's judgment that Reading A is "intolerably clumsy and opaque" belongs to this register, an assessment of intuitive Greek prose rhythm, and Miller himself granted that rhythm-and-balance arguments tend to cancel out.[^metzger] Nässelqvist's 2018 study reopens the question at a register the rhythm-and-balance debate had not reached: the compositional criteria for the ancient colon — the sense-and-breath unit by which Greek prose of the period was actually articulated, requiring syntactical completion and a length of roughly seven to thirty syllables.[^nasselqvist] Measured against those criteria, ὃ γέγονεν at three syllables is too short to close v. 3 as an independent colon under Reading B; under Reading A it opens a proper colon (ὃ γέγονεν ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν, within range), and the gradatio that runs through 1:1–5 is sustained at the boundary rather than broken at it. Nässelqvist's contribution is not an aesthetic refinement of the older rhythm argument but a distinct register of linguistic analysis grounded in the compositional tradition the text itself participates in, and it tips the linguistic side toward Reading A where the field had long held it inconclusive. Metzger's verdict is not thereby refuted — it stands at the level of intuitive prose rhythm on which it was made — but the question it had appeared to foreclose is reopened on criteria it did not engage.

**The patristic structural-break corroboration.** Gregory of Nyssa's text of the verse places a full stop after αὐτῷ in v. 4 — one phrase later than Reading A — and exploits the break polemically against Eunomius. The decisive evidence is argumentative as much as punctuational: Gregory's sequential argument at *Contra Eunomium* 3.10.26 moves λόγος → ζωή → φῶς → σὰρξ ἐγένετο, taking "Life" as a title given to the Word at v. 4, which is intelligible only if ὃ γέγονεν opens v. 4 rather than closing v. 3.[^gregory] Gregory's specific punctuation is not the project's, and his break falls one phrase later than Reading A locates it; what the Nyssen witness corroborates is the early Greek-patristic sensitivity to a structural break in the 1:3/4 region — and Reading A locates that break one phrase earlier, after οὐδὲ ἕν, the very word fixed above.

**The case for Reading B, held against.** Reading B is not a position to be dismissed. It is printed by the THGNT, was the UBS committee's preference, and carries the two weightiest modern defenses of either reading. Miller's is theological-soteriological: under Reading B, ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν is a self-contained predication of the Word's eternal divine life, opening the unit's soteriology directly without the intervening backward reference to "what has come into being." Ridderbos follows Miller on this line, adopting Reading B on the same salvation-historical ground.[^ridderbos] Schnackenburg's is exegetical-lexical and the stronger of the two: granting that "historically, the second alternative" — Reading A — "is preferable" on the textual evidence, and holding the grammatical evidence neutral, he keeps ὃ γέγονεν with οὐδὲ ἕν on the ground that Reading A forces a fluid notion of ζωή at v. 4a, requiring the term to hold together the divine-spiritual life of the Word and the life-principle of all created things.[^schn-b] The objection is real, and it is answered not at the boundary but at the lexical resolution of ζωή on which it depends: the joint reading of ζωή at v. 4a — the resolution of the following unit — is the natural resolution of the term's lexical range, not the unstable compromise the objection supposes, and under it Reading A's syntactic frame is coherent without forcing the term. The two defenders operate at distinct registers and are answered at distinct registers; their cases are held as defensible alternatives, not refuted positions, and a reader who weights the theological-soteriological register (Miller) or the lexical register on ζωή (Schnackenburg) above the colometric may reasonably prefer Reading B.

**Resolution.** The project reads Reading A and rates its confidence moderate-to-high. The "high" component rests on the cumulative weight of the manuscript record, the second-century patristic consensus, Miller's own textual concession, and the colometric argument, which together make a stronger case than Reading B sustains at the levels on which the textual and linguistic sides operate. The "moderate" component is the honest representation of the stylistic state given by the most recent critical-edition engagement: Houghton's 2025 companion presents the question even-handedly — naming the chiastic structure with πάντα as a stylistic warrant for Reading A and the Johannine preference for clauses opening with ἐν plus a demonstrative pronoun as a stylistic warrant for Reading B — and declines to adjudicate between them.[^houghton] Nässelqvist's argument tips the linguistic balance but does not eliminate the stylistic warrants that remain on both sides, and two first-rank commentators have produced sustained defenses of Reading B at registers the colometric argument does not foreclose. What would shift the resolution is correspondingly specific: a published colometric defense of Reading B that engages Nässelqvist directly at the colon-criteria register; a revision of the NA/UBS apparatus that reverses the long-standing committee preference; a new pre-fourth-century papyrus bearing an unambiguous punctuation mark at the boundary; or a major-commentary engagement at a register the question has not yet taken up. Absent these, Reading A stands as the working text, and ὃ γέγονεν opens v. 4 as the syntactic hinge from the Word's creative agency to the life that what-has-come-to-be has in him — the third term of the macro-tense spine and the v. 3 → v. 4 pivot developed in the exegesis of that pivot.
