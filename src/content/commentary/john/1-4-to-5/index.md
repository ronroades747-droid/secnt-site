The unit is John 1:4–5, read on the working text and punctuation this commentary has settled — the reading under which the words ὃ γέγονεν close verse 3 and carry across the sentence-boundary as the subject of what follows:[^text]

> ⁴ … ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν, καὶ ἡ ζωὴ ἦν τὸ φῶς τῶν ἀνθρώπων· ⁵ καὶ τὸ φῶς ἐν τῇ σκοτίᾳ φαίνει, καὶ ἡ σκοτία αὐτὸ οὐ κατέλαβεν.
>
> ⁴ … in him was life, and the life was the light of men; ⁵ and the light shines in the darkness, and the darkness did not overcome it.

On this reading the two verses do not open a new subject; they continue one. They stand on the far side of [the creational pivot at verse 3 to verse 4](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/13-creational-pivot/), where the perfect ὃ γέγονεν turns from creation *through* the Word — πάντα δι' αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο, all things came to be through him — to the *resulting state* of all that has come to be: that it holds its standing, its very life, *in him*. The life of verse 4a is entered across that bridge, not begun anew.

The four clauses that follow are one composed movement. The evangelist has built the unit as a staircase — an *anadiplosis* in which each clause takes up the last significant term of the clause before it and climbs a single step: ζωή (4a) → ἡ ζωή … τὸ φῶς (4b) → τὸ φῶς … τῇ σκοτίᾳ (5a) → ἡ σκοτία … οὐ κατέλαβεν (5b) — **life → light → darkness → the darkness's completed failure.** The figure is not read *into* the text; it is *on* the page, in the catchwords, and it is the settled working text that lets the staircase begin cleanly, its rhythm already visible in the ancient colometry of the manuscripts.[^colometry] Read for its content, the staircase carries a four-step movement, and that movement is the spine of this commentary's exegesis: **ontology** — the eternal life that *was in* the Word (1:4a); **anthropology** — that same life, turned toward humanity, *as* its light (1:4b); **cosmology** — that light *shining in* the world's darkness (1:5a); and **soteriology** — the darkness's failure to *overcome or comprehend* it (1:5b). Each tread is also a seed the Gospel will later grow: the life-giving of the Son at 5:26, the light of the world at 8:12, the light come into a world that loved the darkness at 3:19, and the summons to walk while the light is present at 12:35.[^seedbed]

The whole movement is conditioned by a frame the evangelist has held open since verse 1 — the frame of Genesis. John 1:4–5 is the third sustained engagement with Genesis 1:1–5 in the space of five verses, and it reads that opening in the key of the Word: the creation-light now the light *of men* in the Word, the primeval darkness now a darkness set *against* God.[^genesis] And the movement runs on two dimensions at once. Above is the *synchronic* staircase of terms just named; beneath it runs a *diachronic* sequence of tenses — the imperfect ἦν (twice in verse 4) giving way to the present φαίνει (verse 5a, the Prologue's first present indicative) and then to the aorist κατέλαβεν (verse 5b). How the two interlock is the burden of the commentary's treatment of the unit's [architecture](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/02-gradatio-and-foyer/), laid out before the clause-by-clause exegesis operates on it. One discrimination governs throughout, and is stated here so it need not be re-argued at each step: the unit is **deliberate composition, not deliberate verse.** To say the evangelist composed the staircase and the marked run of tenses is not to reconstruct a hymn or to scan the lines as poetry, but to read the received text as a deliberate final-form work — with the mainstream of recent interpreters who call this material elevated, rhythmical prose.[^rhythmical]

## Why these two verses matter

These two verses are where the Gospel's governing theme — light and darkness — is first sounded, and sounded at its deepest register. Not yet as the drama of belief and unbelief: that is the work of verses 6–13 and of the Gospel's whole body, and it is deliberately *not* this unit's question. Before the light meets any witness or suffers any rejection, John says what the light *is* and *whose* it is — the eternal life in the Word, become the light of humanity — and then, in the unit's last and most weighted word, what the darkness could not do to it: οὐ κατέλαβεν, *it did not overcome — or comprehend — it.*[^katelaben] The passage matters because it sets the terms on which everything the Gospel afterward narrates about light and darkness will run, and because it strikes, already, the first note of the victory the Gospel will tell. It is the protological ground of the light-and-darkness theme, not its resolution: who received the light and who did not is held for what follows — the reception question opening at 1:11, and the one life traced forward to its terminus within the Prologue at [the Word made flesh](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/14-one-life-arc/) in verse 14. For reader and teacher alike the unit sets a single composed arc before both: the life that was *in* the Word is the light of humanity; that light shines — now, and across creation, incarnation, and the present hour — into the darkness; and the darkness has neither mastered nor understood it, and has not overcome it. That arc is the shape of the Gospel's opening.

## How to read this commentary

The commentary is organized in seven movements. The first — this one — establishes the passage: its text, its place in the Prologue, and why it matters. The second records the framework inheritances under which the unit has been read: what the already-settled treatment of John 1:1–3 and the Prologue-wide architecture hand forward to these two verses. The third names the primary sources and the discipline under which they were engaged, including the text-critical judgment that governs the patristic evidence. The fourth articulates the unit's compositional [architecture](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/02-gradatio-and-foyer/) — the synchronic staircase and the diachronic [run of tenses](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/03-macro-tense-run/), held together — as the foundation on which the exegesis rests; it comes before the exegesis because the exegesis leans on it at every verb and at the relation between verse 4 and verse 14. The fifth is the exegesis proper: the [text-critical notes](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/01-the-text/), then the four clauses each treated in turn under the movement they compose — [ontology](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/06-ontology-life-in-him/) (1:4a), [anthropology](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/07-anthropology-light-of-men/) (1:4b), [cosmology](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/08-cosmology-light-in-darkness/) (1:5a), and [soteriology](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/09-soteriology-not-overcome/) (1:5b). The sixth draws out the theological implications as discrete loci. The seventh closes with the unit's [pastoral hearing](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/16-pastoral-hearing/) and its forward arc into the rest of the Prologue and the Gospel. A record of the scholarship engaged, with the works consulted, follows at the close. Throughout, the apparatus is carried in the footnotes, and the readings on which the exposition stands are the project's settled conclusions, set out here rather than re-argued.

## What this reading inherits

This commentary inherits the project's standing framework for John's Prologue, together with the specific conclusions reached at John 1:1–3 that hand directly into these two verses. Naming the inheritance is not a methodological aside but part of the commentary's scholarly disclosure: the reader is shown the framework under which the unit has been read, and the points at which 1:4–5 stands on work already done.

The Prologue is read under the linear, three-movement architecture given its clearest form by Ridderbos and Morris: 1:1–5, the Word in the beginning; 1:6–13, the Word coming as light; 1:14–18, the Word's glory in the flesh. John 1:4–5 completes the first movement — the *foyer* of verses 1–5 — carrying its opening from the Word's [eternal being](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/14-eternal-existence/), his [personal communion with God](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/15-personal-fellowship/), his [full deity](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/16-full-deity/), and his [agency in creation](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/17-creational-agency/) into the life that is *in* him and the light that shines from it. The unit is entered across [the creational pivot](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/13-creational-pivot/) at verse 3 to verse 4, and its diachronic backbone — [the macro-tense spine](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/03-macro-tense-spine/) ἦν / ἐγένετο / ὃ γέγονεν — is likewise inherited and is played out at the unit level in the run of tenses this unit lays down. The working text itself is an inheritance: the reading that alone lets the staircase begin cleanly, which the exegesis does not re-open. The chiastic alternative — held by serious readers, with the Prologue's center set variously at verses 12–13 or at verse 14 — is acknowledged; under it, 1:4–5 would stand within a structural pair rather than close a linear movement. It will be tested deliberately at 1:14, the natural pivot of most chiastic schemes; until then the linear reading, reinforced by the foyer-bracket pattern and the macro-tense spine, stands as the working architecture.[^linear]

The Prologue is read, further, under Carson's image of the *foyer* — the entrance hall through which the reader passes before stepping into the Gospel proper, where what is announced shapes how all that follows is seen. The strong form of the claim — that the Prologue does not merely summarize the Gospel's themes but *originates* them — is held provisionally, and John 1:4–5 contributes substantially to it: the unit sounds the light-and-darkness theme for the first time, and does so at the foyer's far pole, where the life-and-light of verse 4 answers to the Word-made-flesh whose glory is beheld at verse 14. That the light of verse 4 and the glory of verse 14 are one divine self-disclosure the commentary grounds directly, with Dodd.[^dodd] That verse 4 and verse 14 are a deliberately composed pair — an inclusio framing the Prologue — is held with high but provisional confidence, its confirmation reserved for the commentary's later engagement with verse 14. The strong form of the foyer reading therefore stands, after this unit, at high but provisional strength.

The exegesis treats the unit on its own terms, permitting forward glances only where they are exegetically required. Four are made, each the down-payment of a seed the unit plants and the Gospel later grows: 5:26, the Son granted to have life in himself, develops the life *in* the Word; 8:12, ἐγώ εἰμι τὸ φῶς τοῦ κόσμου, is the personal realization of the light *of men*; 3:19, the light come into the world and men loving the darkness, develops the light *shining in* the darkness; and 12:35, walk while you have the light, opens the note of the darkness's non-overcoming.[^forward] The reception drama proper — the true light coming into the world, its non-recognition and non-reception at 1:10–11, the incarnation at 1:14 — is reserved for those passages. In particular, *whether* the light's reception runs first through Israel is not this unit's question and is held for the treatment of 1:11; the universal scope of "the light of men" at 1:4b is not narrowed by that reservation.

The theological implications engage touchpoints beyond the Gospel only where they *substantively illuminate* the unit's claims, not where they merely associate thematically. Genesis 1:1–5 is foundational rather than optional — the creation of light and its separation from darkness, taken up christologically, conditioning the whole movement. Genesis 1:26–27, the image of God, grounds the sense in which the light is "of men" in the possessive as well as the objective register — humanity constituted to receive the light the divine life is. And Psalm 36:9, "in your light we see light," supplies the Old Testament's own life-and-light collocation. Beyond these the theological work resists expansion; the unit's theology is deep at a few points rather than diffuse across many, and cross-canonical material enters only after the exegesis within John is done.

The Greek reception of the Prologue is load-bearing for the project's distinctive contribution. Gregory of Nyssa read John 1:1, 1:4–5, 1:9, 1:14, and 1:18 as one connected sequence of Word, life, light, and only-begotten — early evidence that the Prologue was received as a connected whole — and at verse 5 he read the singular aorist ἔλαμψε(ν) against the φαίνει of all New Testament manuscripts.[^nyssen] This bears on how the unit's architecture was received, not on the working text, which stands. Here the commentary's supersession principle operates: the editor's own collation of the Nyssen text is engaged as primary-text work, taking precedence — at the loci where it is original — over the published secondary treatment of the same witness. Origen's deliberate life-then-light ordering and the fork in the early reception of κατέλαβεν are engaged at the clause level through Heine's edition and the Ancient Christian Commentary.[^origen]

This commentary is a permanent scholarly artifact, and it is held open to revision as the Prologue-wide work is completed. Its architectural claims in particular are held provisionally and are expected to strengthen as the later engagement with 1:14 and 1:15–18 fills in the Prologue's whole shape. The discipline requires no ongoing discussion in the text; it is simply the frame under which the commentary is written and refined.

## The sources and the discipline

The source engagement is layered, and at every resolution the layer at which a source is brought to bear is disclosed. Full substantive engagement with a source read directly at the point where it bears load is the highest register; a source may instead be engaged directly but in a supporting role — a critical apparatus, a reference grammar, a standard reference work — or through its own summary articulation, or through another scholar's citation, or substantively on an adjacent question while remaining at apparatus level on the one at issue. Naming these layers lets the reader see, at each conclusion, both which sources bear on it and what kind of evidence each supplies. A full inventory by layer accompanies the [engagement with prior scholarship](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/15-engagement-prior-scholarship/) at the close.

The load-bearing modern source across the unit is Schnackenburg, whose Prologue chapter is engaged on the sense of ζωή, the sense of φῶς, and the strophe analyses surveyed at the architecture.[^schnack] Carson supplies the seed-bed grid that governs the movement and the reading of the linked clauses as rhythmical prose.[^carson] Ridderbos is the strongest single witness that the run from imperfect to present is *composed*, reading the imperfects of verses 1–4 as the "grand background" for the present of verse 5.[^ridderbos] McHugh supplies the "uncreated life" reading of ζωή, the φῶς κατ' ἐξοχήν of verse 4b, the present φαίνει as "shining perpetually," and a survey of the reconstructions.[^mchugh] Brown supplies the "staircase parallelism" characterization; Barrett the "unified rhythmical prose" verdict, and is engaged-against at the aorist κατέλαβεν; Bultmann names the compositional mechanism of couplet chain-locking, received at the final-form level with its hymn-source explanation surveyed but not adopted; Michaels marks the shift from imperfect to present as the writer returning to his own time.[^field] Nässelqvist and Ed. L. Miller are engaged directly on the colometric and staircase question, and Waetjen anchors the aspect of the run — φαίνει as "a continuous actuality" dominant over the constative aorist — with the honest boundary that he himself disclaims all staircase analysis, so that he anchors the tenses and not the figure.[^tenses] Westcott supplies the present-continuous reading of φαίνει; Dodd the light-and-glory content of the verse 4 / verse 14 pair; Boyarin the reading whose chain of catchwords is received as evidence of an integrated composition; Sosa Siliezar the creation-imagery register; and Calvin, read directly, is foundational to the theology.[^others]

The patristic apparatus is engaged at two registers. The editor's 1974 collation of Gregory of Nyssa's text of John is engaged as primary-text work; the published secondary treatment of the same witness is engaged at its own register and superseded where its reconstruction dilutes the connected-unit reception and rejects the singular ἔλαμψε.[^patristic] Origen is engaged through Heine and the Ancient Christian Commentary at the level of the clause.

The text-critical and grammatical apparatus grounds the isolated decisions the exegesis composes. Houghton grounds the decision for the imperfect ἦν over the present ἐστιν at verse 4a — at the editors' highest level of confidence — and the perfect-to-imperfect seam at verse 3 to verse 4; Abbott supplies the distinction between ἐγένετο and γέγονεν at the seam and the adversative καί at verse 5; and the aspectual apparatus — Robertson, Zerwick, and Harris's Exegetical Guide — supplies the parsings that the run of tenses draws together.[^grammar] The commentary honors the places where a standard authority is silent: no claim about the composed run or the staircase is routed to a grammar or study that does not in fact treat the verse.

The theological loci are drawn in the Reformed-tradition register. Calvin is read directly at every clause and is foundational to the implications, including the reading of the darkened understanding in the σκοτία; Bavinck supplies the systematic register for the image of God at the locus where the light is "of men" in its constitutive sense.[^reformed]

Four disciplines govern the composition. The first is *final-form composition*: the unit is read as the evangelist's deliberate finished work, and the various hymn- and strophe-reconstructions are surveyed at the diachronic register but not adopted. The second follows from it — *receive the observation, decline the explanation*: where a source's compositional observation is sound but its account of how the text came to be lies off the project's register, the observation is received at the final-form level and the explanation set aside, so that Bultmann's chain-locking and Boyarin's catchword chain are received as evidence of integrated composition while their cultic-hymn and midrashic-genre explanations are declined. The third is the *co-primary discrimination*: the synchronic staircase and the diachronic run of tenses are two dimensions of one composition, held together and separated only by register, so that neither borrows the other's witnesses — the figure anchored by Nässelqvist and Miller, the tenses by Ridderbos and Waetjen. The fourth is a discipline of *sourcing*: each source is engaged only where the record shows it actually treats the question, and the places where a standard authority is silent are honored rather than filled. Above all, the discrimination stated at the outset holds throughout: the unit is deliberate composition, not deliberate verse.
