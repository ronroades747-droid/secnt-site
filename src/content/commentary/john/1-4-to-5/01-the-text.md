The exegesis of these two verses proceeds through their four clauses in the order the evangelist composed them, and it proceeds on a fixed text. This first section establishes that text. The unit's text-critical situation is unusual in a clarifying way: nearly all of it is settled before the unit begins, inherited from the reading of John 1:1–3, and only one point of decision belongs to the unit itself. Naming which is which is part of the commentary's discipline of disclosure — the reader is shown where the working text rests on judgments made earlier and where it rests on a judgment made here.

## The boundary the unit inherits

Everything in verses 4 and 5 stands on a decision about where one sentence ends and the next begins — the placement of the full stop in the stretch χωρὶς αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο οὐδὲ ἕν· ὃ γέγονεν ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν. The commentary reads the break after οὐδὲ ἕν, so that verse 3 closes as a complete negative universal — *without him not even one thing came into being* — and the relative clause ὃ γέγονεν, *what has come into being*, opens verse 4 as the subject of what follows: *what has come into being — in him was life.* The alternative places the stop one clause later, after ὃ γέγονεν, attaching the relative to verse 3 (*not even one thing came into being that has come into being*) and leaving verse 4 to open with a self-contained ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν.[^boundary]

This is the reading settled in the commentary's treatment of John 1:1–3 and carried here, not reopened. It was argued there on the convergence of the oldest witnesses, which mark a sense-pause after οὐδὲ ἕν, with the compositional evidence that the passage's step-by-step linkage — the *gradatio* by which each clause takes up the last word of the one before — begins cleanly only under this division: ὃ γέγονεν ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν stands as a balanced colon with ζωή at its close, handing directly to καὶ ἡ ζωὴ ἦν τὸ φῶς, whereas the alternative leaves ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν too short to stand on its own and blunts the repetition of ζωή. The reading is not without able defenders on the other side, and their case is a serious one engaged in full where the boundary was decided; that ὃ γέγονεν belongs with what follows was, in fact, the reading of every Christian writer down to about the year 300.[^boundary]

What the unit takes from that settled boundary is a subject and a grammar. The perfect ὃ γέγονεν is read as a *resultative* perfect — it names not the act of coming-to-be but the standing result of it, created reality in its finished state — and it stands as the subject of which the clause predicates life: *what has come into being — in him was life.* The prepositional phrase ἐν αὐτῷ, *in him*, carries the weight, marking the Word as the locus in which all that has come to be holds its life; this is the turn from creation *through* the Word (πάντα δι' αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο) to the life of the created order *in* him that the earlier cycle fixed at [the pivot from verse 3 to verse 4](/commentary/john/1-1-to-3/13-creational-pivot/). On this base the exegesis of the first clause operates; the *force* of the verb and the *sense* of ζωή are the questions of the [ontology](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/06-ontology-life-in-him/) that follows, not of the text-critical note, which settles only the parse.

## The verb of verse 4a: ἦν, not ἐστιν

The one variant intrinsic to this unit is the verb of verse 4a. The manuscripts divide between the imperfect ζωὴ ἦν, *in him* ***was*** *life*, and the present ζωὴ ἐστιν, *in him* ***is*** *life*. The commentary reads the imperfect, and here — unlike the boundary, where the field remains genuinely divided — the decision is as firm as text-critical decisions become.[^verb]

The external evidence is decisive. Almost every Greek witness reads the imperfect ἦν, including the oldest papyri and the great fourth- and fifth-century codices; the editors of the standard critical text rate the reading at their highest level of certainty. The present ἐστιν is real and early — it is carried by Sinaiticus and Bezae, by much of the Old Latin, the Old Syriac, and the Sahidic, and by several early writers outside the mainstream — but it is best explained as a secondary smoothing. A scribe who has just read the perfect γέγονεν feels the friction of the imperfect ἦν standing beside it and eases it into a present. This is why the boundary and the verb are, at bottom, one piece of evidence seen from two sides: it is precisely the adopted punctuation, which sets the perfect γέγονεν in the same sentence as the copula, that makes the imperfect the harder reading and so the original one.[^verb]

The grammatical objection that once seemed to favor the present — that a perfect ought to be followed by a present, not an imperfect — dissolves once ὃ γέγονεν is read as a resultative perfect: a form naming a standing result sits without strain beside a following imperfect. And two further considerations settle the matter. The imperfect is required by the clause that follows it: the second half of verse 4, καὶ ἡ ζωὴ ἦν τὸ φῶς τῶν ἀνθρώπων, carries its own ἦν, and *the second* ἦν *requires the first* — the parallel would break if verse 4a read a present. The return of the imperfect here is also the completion of the pattern of tenses the whole Prologue has been running, the point at which the eternal-being *was* is sounded again before the passage turns, at verse 5, to its first present; that pattern is the subject of the unit's [architecture](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/03-macro-tense-run/). Finally, the decision does not depend on the punctuation at all: one of the ablest defenders of attaching ὃ γέγονεν to verse 3 nonetheless keeps the imperfect and rejects the present, so a reader on either side of the boundary question arrives at the same verb.[^verb]

## The text of verse 5, and a patristic reading worth recording

Verse 5 raises no textual question at all in the Greek New Testament tradition. The present φαίνει, *shines*, and the aorist κατέλαβεν, *did not overcome* — or *comprehend* — stand uncontested; the critical apparatus records no variation unit in the verse. The working text of the whole unit is therefore secure at every point but the one adjudicated above.

One reading from the history of interpretation is worth recording, not because it disturbs the text but because it illuminates how the clause was heard. Gregory of Nyssa, in the fourth century, cites verse 5a with a singular aorist — ἔλαμψε(ν), *the light* ***shone*** *in the darkness* — where every New Testament manuscript reads the present φαίνει. The reading is original to Gregory, established from his own citations rather than from the later manuscript tradition, and it is evidence that the clause could be received early as naming a decisive, completed shining rather than a continuous one.[^v5] The commentary's working text remains φαίνει; the interest of the Nyssen ἔλαμψε lies not in the text it would replace but in what it shows about the reading of the tenses — a bearing taken up where the unit treats the light [shining in the darkness](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/08-cosmology-light-in-darkness/) and the darkness that [did not overcome it](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/09-soteriology-not-overcome/), not here.

With the working text fixed — ὃ γέγονεν opening verse 4 as subject, the imperfect ἦν established against the present, and verse 5 uncontested — the exegesis can turn to what the evangelist has built on it: first the unit's [compositional architecture](/commentary/john/1-4-to-5/02-gradatio-and-foyer/), the staircase of terms and the run of tenses held together, and then the four clauses in turn.
