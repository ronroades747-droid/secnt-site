# The Text of John in Gregory of Nyssa — A Reader's Orientation

*Ronald E. Roades, "The Text of the Gospel of John in the Writings of Gregory of Nyssa." MA thesis, Wheaton College, June 1974.*

**The question.** What text of the Fourth Gospel did Gregory of Nyssa actually read? Gregory never copied out a manuscript of John, but he quoted, paraphrased, and adapted it throughout his theological works. The thesis recovers the Gospel text lying behind those references — Gregory's own biblical exemplar — and then asks where that text belongs in the manuscript tradition: which of the great text-types (Neutral, Caesarean, Western, Byzantine) does Gregory's John most resemble?

**Why it is worth doing.** Patristic citations rank below the manuscripts and versions as witnesses to the New Testament text, yet the thesis holds them invaluable for something the manuscripts cannot supply: a Father can be localized and dated, so his text helps place and date the readings he carries. The difficulty is that one must read *through* a Father — through his habits of loose quotation and through the later scribes who recopied his works — to reach the text he was citing. Recovering that exemplar is not a matter of clipping quotations and arranging them in order; the distortions have to be controlled for first.

**The method.** Roades adopts the newer, more rigorous methodology for analyzing patristic citations, over against the older methods the thesis judges inadequate (a critique it shares with Gordon Fee). Gregory's references are gathered from the critical edition — *Gregorii Nysseni Opera* (Jaeger/Langerbeck) as the primary witness, Migne's *Patrologia Graeca* only where it is the sole witness — and laid out verse by verse across John 1–21. A controlled sigla system distinguishes full citation from partial citation from adaptation, and verbal correspondence from probable derivation, so the weight of each witness stays visible. The reconstructed text is then collated against representatives of each text-type and the agreements tallied statistically (with the aid of a digital computer, ch. IV), with Theodoret of Cyrus held alongside for comparison.

**What the collation establishes.** Gregory's text of John is mainly Byzantine — but less thoroughly Byzantine than, for instance, Theodoret's — and it carries a definite strain of Neutral readings that may reflect an early stage in the growth of the Byzantine text-type. The text-type furthest from Gregory is Western; the pull away from Byzantine runs toward the Neutral, not the Western (here differing from von Soden), and no particular affinity with the "purple" codices was found. Precise placement, the thesis allows, must await definitive work on the Caesarean text in John and on Chrysostom's affinities.
